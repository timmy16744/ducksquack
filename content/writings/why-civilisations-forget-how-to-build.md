---
title: "Why Civilisations Forget How to Build"
date: "2018-03-15"
color: "orange"
---

The Sydney light rail project was supposed to cost $1.6 billion and take four years. It will cost over $3 billion and take considerably longer. The disruption to the city during construction has been extraordinary. Businesses along the route have closed. Traffic patterns have been upended for years. The project has become, in the Australian tradition, a political football, with blame distributed according to tribal affiliation rather than competence.

I mention this not because it is unusual but because it is typical. In Berlin, the Brandenburg Airport was supposed to open in 2011. It opened in 2020, at roughly three times the original budget. California's high-speed rail was estimated at $33 billion in 2008. The current estimate exceeds $100 billion, and the project may never be completed. The pattern is global. It is consistent. It is getting worse.

Something has happened to our ability to build things. And I think the something is more interesting than bad management or corruption, though both are present. I think we are watching a civilisational process, one that has historical precedents, and the precedents are not encouraging.


Rome built the aqueducts in the centuries of its expansion. The engineering was extraordinary for its time and remains impressive today. Water flowed across valleys and through mountains, carried by precisely graded channels over distances of a hundred kilometres, arriving at its destination through gravity alone. The system sustained a city of over a million people.

By the fifth century, Rome could no longer maintain them. Not because the engineering knowledge had been suddenly lost in some catastrophe. But because the institutional capacity to organise complex projects had degraded. The administrative apparatus that coordinated labour, allocated resources, maintained standards, and sustained the political will for long-term infrastructure investment had slowly, over generations, become less capable.

The aqueducts crumbled not because nobody understood how water flows but because nobody could organise enough people, with enough resources, over enough time, to keep them running. The knowledge was institutional, not individual. It resided in systems, not in heads. When the systems degraded, the knowledge became inaccessible even though it had not technically been lost.

I think about this when I read about the Sydney light rail.


The pattern, as I observe it, has a consistent shape.

Early in a civilisation's trajectory, building is relatively simple. Not easy, nothing about constructing an aqueduct or a railroad is easy, but simple in the sense that the path from decision to completion is relatively direct. A ruler or a government decides that something should be built. Resources are allocated. Labour is organised. The thing is built.

As the civilisation matures, layers of process accumulate. Safety regulations, which are individually reasonable. Environmental assessments, which are individually necessary. Community consultations, which are individually democratic. Legal frameworks for dispute resolution, which are individually just. Labour agreements, which are individually fair.

Each layer is added for good reasons, usually in response to a genuine failure. A building collapses, so building codes are strengthened. A community is harmed by a project, so consultation requirements are introduced. Workers are exploited, so protections are mandated. Each addition solves a real problem.

But the layers accumulate. They interact in ways that nobody designed and nobody fully understands. The path from decision to completion becomes longer, more expensive, more uncertain. The cost of compliance rises. The timeline extends. The number of potential points of failure multiplies.

At some threshold, and I do not know where the threshold is, only that it exists, the accumulated process becomes the primary obstacle to building. The system designed to ensure that things are built well becomes a system that ensures things are not built at all.


I have watched this in Adelaide with a specificity that statistics cannot capture.

I have sat in meetings where a project that should take months was mapped onto a timeline of years. Not because the engineering was complex. Not because the funding was unavailable. Because the approvals process required environmental assessment, heritage review, traffic study, community consultation, acoustic assessment, visual impact assessment, each one taking months, each one potentially generating objections that require further assessment, each one adding cost and uncertainty to a project whose merits were never in dispute.

The people administering these processes are not incompetent. They are often conscientious, doing exactly what the regulations require them to do. The regulations themselves are not unreasonable in isolation. The problem is not any individual rule or any individual administrator. The problem is the system, the accumulated weight of individually defensible requirements that collectively prevent action.

I have travelled enough to know that this is not universal. In Tokyo, infrastructure projects of extraordinary complexity are completed on time and on budget with a regularity that would seem fictional to an Australian observer. The Shinkansen network was built in the 1960s at a speed and scale that would be literally impossible under current Australian regulatory frameworks. Japan is not a lawless country. It has regulations, standards, environmental protections. But it has also maintained the institutional capacity to organise complex building projects and see them through to completion.

The difference is not cultural in any mysterious sense. It is institutional. Japan has institutions that work. They coordinate. They decide. They execute. The path from decision to completion, while not without obstacles, remains navigable. The process serves the project rather than consuming it.


Why does this happen? Why do civilisations that once built magnificently lose the ability to build at all?

I think the answer lies in what I would call institutional sediment. Every institution accumulates process over time, like sediment in a river. Early in the institution's life, the river flows freely. The channel is clear. Decisions move from conception to execution with relative speed. But each year adds a thin layer of sediment, a new regulation, a new reporting requirement, a new approval step, a new compliance obligation. Each layer is thin. Each is individually invisible. Over decades, the river silts up.

The people working within the institution do not notice the silting because it happens too slowly. Each year looks much like the previous year. The additional process is marginal. The extra form, the additional sign-off, the new consultation requirement, these are absorbed into routine without comment. It is only when you compare the current state to the state of twenty or thirty or fifty years ago that the scale of the accumulation becomes visible.

By that point, reform is extraordinarily difficult. Every layer of sediment has a constituency. The environmental assessment process employs environmental consultants. The community consultation process employs community engagement specialists. The compliance framework employs compliance officers. To remove any layer is to threaten the livelihoods of the people who administer it, and those people will resist. They will resist not because they are villains but because they are human beings protecting their incomes and their sense of professional identity.

This is why the problem is civilisational rather than managerial. It cannot be solved by better project management or smarter ministers. It is a structural feature of mature institutional systems. The sediment accumulates because there is no natural mechanism for its removal. Adding process is easy, it responds to a visible failure and creates a constituency. Removing process is hard, it requires overcoming a constituency and risks an invisible failure.


I do not know where this leads. The optimistic reading is that technology provides a mechanism for cutting through institutional sediment. Automation, artificial intelligence, digital government, these could, in principle, reduce the cost of compliance and accelerate the path from decision to completion.

The pessimistic reading is that technology adds its own layers of sediment. Digital systems create digital compliance requirements. Automated processes require automated oversight. The complexity shifts to a new medium but does not diminish.

What I observe today, from Adelaide, is a country that is visibly losing the ability to build. Not because we lack resources. Not because we lack engineers. Not because we lack political will, though that is debatable. Because the accumulated weight of our institutional processes has made building so expensive, so slow, and so uncertain that rational actors increasingly choose not to try.

The Romans did not wake up one morning unable to maintain their aqueducts. The capacity degraded gradually, over generations, so slowly that each generation could tell itself that things were roughly as they had always been. By the time the degradation was undeniable, the capacity to reverse it had degraded as well.

I look at the Sydney light rail. I look at the project timelines, the cost overruns, the endless process. And I wonder whether we are watching the same pattern unfold, in a different century, in a different country, with the same result.

The inability to build is not a policy failure. It is a diagnostic. It tells you something about the stage of the civilisation you are in. And what it tells you is worth paying attention to, even if the message is uncomfortable.
