---
title: "Energy as Leverage"
date: "2022-03-15"
color: "red"
---

Russia invaded Ukraine three weeks ago. In the time since, I have watched Europe discover something that should not have required a war to reveal: energy dependence is strategic dependence, and strategic dependence is leverage, and leverage, once exercised, is extremely difficult to escape.

Germany built its industrial economy on cheap Russian gas. This was presented, for decades, as smart economic policy. Diversification of energy supply. Engagement through trade. The theory that economic interdependence would moderate Russian behaviour, that a country selling gas to Europe would not attack a European neighbour because the economic consequences would be too severe.

The theory has been tested. It was wrong.

Russia has invaded a European country while continuing to sell gas to the continent that is, nominally, opposing the invasion. The gas still flows through Ukrainian pipelines, a surreal detail that captures the absurdity of the arrangement. Europe funds the war machine through energy purchases and then sanctions the war machine through financial restrictions, and the net effect is a kind of strategic incoherence that would be darkly comic if people were not dying.

The lesson is not about Russia specifically. The lesson is about the category of error: building critical dependence on a strategic competitor and assuming that the competition will never be exercised because the economic relationship is too valuable. This assumption, that economic interdependence prevents conflict, has been the foundation of Western strategic thinking since the end of the Cold War. It is the assumption behind the European gas relationship with Russia. It is the assumption behind the Western economic relationship with China. It is, I would argue, the assumption behind Australia's entire strategic posture.

We export iron ore to China. We export coal, gas, agricultural products, education services. China is our largest trading partner by a substantial margin. The relationship generates enormous wealth, wealth that funds government services and maintains living standards and keeps the housing market inflated.

We also depend on China for manufactured goods. Electronics, pharmaceuticals, medical devices, industrial components, consumer products. The things that fill our homes and power our businesses come, disproportionately, from Chinese factories.

The arrangement is precisely analogous to Germany and Russian gas. We sell raw materials to a strategic competitor. We buy finished goods from the same competitor. We assume that the economic relationship is too valuable for either side to disrupt.

Europe is learning, this month, what happens when that assumption fails. Gas prices have spiked. Energy costs have cascaded through every sector of the European economy. The scramble for alternatives, LNG terminals, coal restarts, nuclear reconsiderations, reveals the depth of the miscalculation. Building energy infrastructure takes years. The dependence was constructed over decades. It cannot be unwound in weeks, no matter how urgent the need.

Australia has not yet had its moment of discovery. The relationship with China has been strained, the trade disputes of 2020, the barley tariffs, the wine restrictions, but the iron ore trade has continued because China needs it as much as we need the revenue. For now.

The question is what happens when the balance shifts. When China develops alternative sources, or reduces demand, or decides to exercise the leverage that our dependence provides. When the strategic competition in the Indo-Pacific intensifies to the point where economic relationships are subordinated to strategic objectives, as they have been in Europe.

Energy is not a commodity. This is the lesson Europe is learning in real time, at extraordinary cost. Energy is leverage. Whoever controls your energy supply controls your options. And by extension: whoever controls the supply of anything you cannot do without controls your options. Energy. Semiconductors. Pharmaceuticals. Manufactured goods. The category is broad, and Australia's position within it is precarious.

I keep waiting for the Australian strategic conversation to absorb this lesson. To look at what is happening in Europe and recognise the pattern. To ask whether our own dependencies, different in specifics, identical in structure, carry the same risks.

I keep waiting. The conversation has not started.

The gas still flows through Ukraine. The iron ore still flows to China. The assumptions remain untested. Europe is learning what happens when the test arrives. The question for Australia is whether we can learn from their experience or whether we require our own.

I suspect I know the answer. I hope I am wrong.
