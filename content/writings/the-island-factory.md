---
title: "The Island Factory"
date: "2021-09-15"
color: "red"
---

There is a factory in Hsinchu, Taiwan, that you have never heard of and that your life depends on. It is operated by the Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company, and it produces the majority of the world's most advanced microchips. The processor in your phone was almost certainly fabricated there. The chips in your car, your laptop, the servers that run your bank, the systems that manage air traffic control and power grids and hospital equipment, a staggering proportion of them come from fabs on an island of twenty-four million people in the western Pacific.

The semiconductor shortage that has disrupted global industry over the past year has made this dependency visible in a way that it was not previously. Car manufacturers have halted production lines. Consumer electronics companies have delayed launches. The prices of products that depend on chips, which is to say, nearly all products, have risen.

But the shortage is the minor problem. The major problem is the geography.

Taiwan sits in the Taiwan Strait, roughly 160 kilometres from mainland China. China considers Taiwan part of its sovereign territory. The United States has maintained a policy of strategic ambiguity regarding Taiwan's defence, neither explicitly committing to defend the island nor explicitly ruling it out. The military balance in the strait has been shifting steadily in China's favour for decades.

The most critical supply chain on earth passes through the most contested strait on earth. And nobody has a backup plan.

This is not Hormuz. Oil has substitutes, eventually. Renewable energy is advancing. If the Strait of Hormuz were closed, the economic disruption would be severe but the world could adapt because the commodity that flows through it is being superseded by alternatives.

Advanced semiconductors have no substitutes. There is no alternative source. Intel is attempting to rebuild American fabrication capacity, but the effort is years from producing chips at the cutting edge. Samsung has fabs in South Korea, but they do not match TSMC's scale or capability at the most advanced nodes. The concentration of production is so extreme that it constitutes a single point of failure for the entire digital economy.

Consider what a serious disruption would mean. Not a war, necessarily. A blockade. A quarantine zone. The kind of military posture that would make commercial shipping into Taiwanese ports uninsurable. The fabs would not need to be destroyed. They would simply need to be inaccessible.

Within weeks, the global supply of advanced chips would begin to constrict. Within months, production of everything that depends on those chips, cars, phones, medical devices, military systems, industrial equipment, communications infrastructure, would slow or stop. The effects would cascade through the global economy in ways that are difficult to model because the dependencies are so pervasive and so poorly mapped.

For Australia, the implications are acute. We have no semiconductor industry. We have no fab capability. We have no path to one. The chips in our infrastructure, our defence systems, our communications networks, our economy, all of them come from somewhere else. If that somewhere else becomes inaccessible, we have no domestic substitute and no timeline for developing one.

This is the dependency that I keep returning to, the one that seems to me more strategically significant than oil or gas or manufactured goods, because it is more concentrated, more fragile, and more essential. A modern economy without semiconductors is not a poorer economy. It is a non-functional economy. The digital systems on which everything depends simply stop.

I have been to Taiwan. It is a remarkable place, democratic, prosperous, technologically sophisticated, culturally vibrant. The people I met there live with the knowledge that their country exists in a permanent state of strategic uncertainty. They have built something extraordinary under conditions that would paralyse most societies. The fact that the global digital economy depends on their continued stability is not something most of them take for granted. They understand their vulnerability better than anyone.

The question I cannot answer, and that I have not heard anyone in Australian policy circles answer convincingly, is what we do about this. The dependency is real. The risk is real. The timeline for developing alternatives is measured in decades. And the geopolitical dynamics that could trigger a crisis are accelerating, not stabilising.

We have built a civilisation on a technology whose production is concentrated on a single island in a contested sea. This is either the greatest strategic vulnerability in modern history or it is not. I think it is. And the absence of serious discussion about it, in Australia or anywhere else, is a silence that concerns me more the longer it persists.
