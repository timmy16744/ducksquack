I. Every Fifty Years, the World's Money Breaks

Here is something they do not teach you in economics, or in history, or in any discipline that would otherwise seem obligated to mention it. Every major monetary system in the last 150 years has shared a single characteristic: it lasted exactly as long as the economic reality that created it. The moment the physical basis of the global economy shifted, the currency standard shifted with it.

Not gradually. Not by polite negotiation between finance ministers. It broke. Usually under the pressure of a war or a fiscal crisis. And something new, something that better reflected what the economy had actually become, emerged from the wreckage.

Follow this pattern with me. It takes about three minutes to trace, and if you see it clearly, the rest of this essay will feel less like speculation and more like extrapolation.

Gold: 1870s to 1914

The late nineteenth century. Every major industrial power anchors its currency to gold. One ounce equals a fixed number of units of any given currency. It works because gold is genuinely scarce, portable, universally recognised. London sits at the centre. The system runs for decades because the underlying economic reality (land, agriculture, early industrialisation) is stable enough to support it.

Then the First World War arrives. Governments need to spend far beyond what their gold reserves can back. So they print. And the gold standard, which had seemed as permanent as gravity, does not evolve or transition. It collapses. One day it is the architecture of civilisation. The next it is an artefact.

Bretton Woods: 1944 to 1971

Forty-four nations at a hotel in New Hampshire. A new design. The US dollar pegged to gold at $35 per ounce. Every other major currency pegged to the dollar. It works because the United States holds most of the world's gold and dominates global manufacturing. The dollar becomes the anchor because American productive capacity makes it credible.

It lasts twenty-seven years. Persistent deficits, the cost of Vietnam, expanding domestic programmes. The United States is spending money it does not have. Foreign nations begin demanding gold for their surplus dollars. There is not enough. On August 15th, 1971, Richard Nixon closes the gold window. A system that had governed global finance for a generation ends, effectively, in an afternoon. No vote. No transition plan. Just a president on television telling the world the rules had changed.

The Petrodollar: 1973 to Present

Now the dollar is floating freely, backed by nothing physical, and the question is obvious: why would anyone keep using it? The answer comes from Henry Kissinger and a handshake with Saudi Arabia. Saudi Arabia prices all oil exports in US dollars. The United States provides military protection to the Gulf states.

Because every economy on Earth needs oil, every economy on Earth now needs dollars. The dollar's dominance is no longer maintained by gold or by American manufacturing superiority. It is maintained by energy. By the military-enforced monopoly on the denomination of the energy trade.

This is the system you and I still live inside. And by the logic of its own internal structure, it is already obsolete.

I am aware that is a large claim. Let me show you why.

II. Three Curves That Change Everything

Every transition from one monetary era to the next has required the same catalyst: a shift in what constitutes the binding scarce resource of economic production. To understand why the petrodollar era is ending, you need to look at three cost curves that are collapsing simultaneously. Each, on its own, would be a major economic event. Together, they constitute something without precedent: the simultaneous zeroing-out of the three fundamental inputs to production.

Intelligence. Labour. Energy.

These are not projections. They are documented trends, visible in corporate filings and academic literature, that have been unfolding for years largely beneath the attention of mainstream economic commentary. The remarkable thing is not that they are happening. The remarkable thing is that so few people seem to have worked through what they mean when they converge.

Intelligence

The cost of training artificial intelligence has declined at roughly fifty times the rate predicted by Moore's Law. Moore's Law itself describes one of the most powerful deflationary forces in economic history. Think about what it means for something to make Moore's Law look slow.

ResNet-50, a standard AI training benchmark, cost approximately $1,000 to train in 2017. By 2019 that figure was $10. Today it is measured in cents. On the inference side (the deployment of AI to actually do useful work) costs have dropped roughly tenfold per year. GPT-4 level capability cost $20 per million tokens in late 2022. By 2025 it cost 40 cents. A fifty-fold reduction in three years. The Chinese lab DeepSeek trained their R1 model for approximately $6 million while Western competitors were spending hundreds of millions for comparable performance.

The practical implication, once you see it, is impossible to unsee: cognitive labour (the capacity to analyse, reason, write, code, decide) is approaching zero marginal cost. Every white-collar function that can be decomposed into information processing is experiencing the same deflationary pressure that manufacturing underwent when it moved to Asia. But faster. And without geographical constraint. And without slowing down.

Physical Labour

Industrial robots currently operate at approximately 75 cents per hour in electricity and maintenance. A human worker in a developed economy costs fifteen to twenty dollars per hour. Credible projections have the robot figure at 20 cents per hour by 2032.

The bigger development is general-purpose humanoid robots. Tesla's Optimus platform is targeted at $20,000 to $25,000 per unit. At those economics, each robot replaces roughly $57,000 per year in human labour costs while leasing at five to ten dollars per hour. The global physical labour market is approximately $40 trillion annually. Construction, agriculture, logistics, healthcare, manufacturing. Anything performed with human hands is exposed to this.

Energy

Solar module costs have declined 99.6 percent since 1976. From $106 per watt to 38 cents per watt. Solar is already cheaper than building new natural gas capacity without subsidies. It is approaching the operating costs of existing gas plants.

The mechanism is Wright's Law: for every doubling of installed solar capacity, module costs fall another 20 percent. This relationship has held for fifty years without interruption. There is no physical or economic reason to expect it to stop. The cost trajectory of solar and the cost trajectory of fossil fuels are moving in permanently opposite directions. This is not a cycle. It is a crossing, and we are somewhere in the middle of it.

What Happens When They Meet

Now here is where it gets interesting. Run these three curves to their conclusions simultaneously. If intelligence is free, and physical labour is free, and energy is nearly free, then the only remaining scarce inputs to economic production are mass (raw materials) and energy itself. Everything else becomes software: infinitely replicable at near-zero marginal cost.

You cannot copy a kilowatt hour. You cannot duplicate a tonne of lithium. But you can run the same piece of code a billion times for the cost of the electricity to power the servers. In the world these curves are creating, the scarce things are the physical things. And the most universally scarce, most universally necessary physical thing is energy.

This is the point in the argument where a reasonable person might say: alright, that is a nice framework, but it is still theoretical. Where is the physical evidence that anyone is actually building toward this?

I'm glad you asked.

III. The Infrastructure Is Already Under Construction

Orbital Solar

Ground-based solar faces three constraints that no amount of engineering can fully solve. The atmosphere absorbs roughly 60 percent of incoming solar radiation. Weather creates intermittency. Night exists. An array in sun-synchronous low Earth orbit faces none of these.

The numbers are worth pausing on. Space receives approximately 1,400 watts per square metre, constant and unfiltered. Ground arrays peak at 1,000 on a clear day and average far less. Space-grade photovoltaic panels operate at 40 to 50 percent efficiency versus 20 to 25 percent terrestrial. The capacity factor (the percentage of time actually generating) exceeds 95 percent in orbit versus 20 to 30 percent on the ground. Combined, an orbital installation generates roughly thirteen times more energy per unit area than an identical ground installation. That is not an improvement on the existing paradigm. That is a different paradigm.

The Filing That Nobody Talked About

In January 2026, SpaceX filed with the United States Federal Communications Commission for permission to launch up to one million satellites. Not internet relays. Orbital data centres: solar-powered compute nodes in space. The FCC Space Bureau accepted the filing within five days.

The filing's stated purpose deserves more attention than it received. SpaceX described the constellation as a first step toward becoming a Kardashev Level Two civilisation: one capable of harnessing the full power output of its star. This language appeared not in a blog post or a conference talk but in a document submitted to a United States federal regulatory body. Whatever your view of the company or its founder, the stated intent is on the public record.

The Vertical Integration Question

I want to be careful here, because this is the section most likely to read as either advocacy or alarm depending on your priors, and it is neither. It is an observation about industrial structure.

A small cluster of interconnected companies is assembling what amounts to the complete vertical stack of the next economic era. Solar generation, grid-scale battery storage, electric transport, humanoid robotics, autonomous driving, AI compute, orbital launch infrastructure, and the intelligence layer connecting all of it. You can name the companies. You already have.

Take each piece individually and it is a significant commercial development. Take them together and they represent something with no direct historical precedent: a single interconnected system spanning energy production, energy storage, physical labour, cognitive labour, and compute infrastructure. In the framework this essay describes, whoever controls that stack holds a position structurally analogous to a central bank. They do not need anyone's permission to denominate value. They simply become the entity whose productive capacity everyone else's activity depends on.

Now, this could be wrong. The integration could fragment. Competitors could unbundle the stack. Regulation could intervene. I want to be honest that this is a structural observation, not a prediction of inevitability. But the trend is real, and if you are waiting for a formal announcement that the rules of the global economy are about to change, you have misunderstood how monetary transitions work. They are never announced. They are observed after the fact by historians.

IV. Winners, Losers, and the Map Nobody Wants to Look At

Here is where the argument gets uncomfortable. The petrodollar system has enforced a particular hierarchy of nations for fifty years. An energy-denominated successor system would invert that hierarchy. Not at the margins. Fundamentally. And several of the nations most of us think of as permanently wealthy are, by the physical metrics of the energy era, in serious structural trouble.

Who Wins

China is the most formidable actor in this transition and is systematically underestimated by Western commentary fixated on its property crisis and demographic headwinds. China manufactures approximately 80 percent of the world's solar panels. It holds dominant positions in rare earth elements, lithium, cobalt, and manganese. Its industrial policy has been explicitly oriented toward the energy transition for over a decade. In an energy-denominated world, China's position is extraordinarily strong, and not because of financial manipulation or military coercion, but because it physically controls the supply chain of the transition itself. This is the fact that most Western analysis finds it difficult to sit with.

Australia is, for me, the most painful case to write about. By the physical metrics of the energy era, it should be a significant winner. Extraordinary solar irradiation across the north and west. Large deposits of critical minerals. A land mass relative to population that provides virtually unlimited space for infrastructure. And yet Australia has consistently chosen to be an exporter of raw materials rather than a manufacturer of energy products, repeating the mistake Norway made before it corrected course with its sovereign wealth fund. The window to choose differently exists. It is measured in years, not decades. Whether Australian politics is capable of acting within that window is a question I genuinely do not know the answer to.

Nations with abundant solar and wind resources (North Africa, the American Southwest, the Middle East to the extent it pivots from extraction to generation, parts of South and Central Asia) become the new Saudi Arabias of the energy era. Geography that was economically marginal under the petrodollar becomes a primary productive asset.

Who Loses

The United Kingdom is perhaps the starkest case. Its post-imperial economic identity has been constructed almost entirely on financial services: the City of London, sterling as a secondary reserve currency, the clustering of global capital management. In an energy-denominated world, the ability to manage financial instruments is not a productive asset in the relevant sense. The UK produces little energy, has limited solar resources, has undergone near-complete deindustrialisation, and faces compounding demographic and fiscal pressure. The decline that British citizens are already experiencing, in real wages, in public services, in housing, may not be a policy failure that better governance can fix. It may be the early symptoms of an economic model becoming obsolete before the replacement has been built.

That is a difficult thing to say. It is more difficult to argue against on the merits.

Ireland's transformation over the past thirty years was achieved primarily through tax arbitrage: offering multinationals favourable treatment in exchange for locating European operations on the island. When AI automates the cognitive labour those operations employed, and energy replaces intellectual property as the dominant input to value creation, the basis of that bargain erodes. Ireland's competitive advantage was always more fragile than its GDP figures suggested. The figures were, in a sense, measuring someone else's wealth that happened to be routed through Irish accounting.

The petrostates face an existential question of speed. Saudi Arabia, the UAE, Qatar, and Kuwait are racing between depleting their leverage and building productive capacity for what comes next. They have the financial resources and the solar resources to succeed. Whether political structures built around oil-funded patronage can adapt fast enough is the open variable. The Vision 2030 programmes are not vanity projects. They are survival strategies.

The United States: Both Sides of the Ledger

The United States occupies perhaps the strangest position of any nation in this framework: simultaneously the country with the most to lose from a transition that displaces the petrodollar, and the country whose private sector is most aggressively building the replacement.

Chip export controls on China, tariffs on Chinese solar panels, the regulatory treatment of SpaceX's orbital filings: these can be read as either protection of legacy interests or positioning for the next era, depending on which part of the American state you think is driving them. The incoherence is genuine. It reflects a real internal conflict over which future America is building.

If American-controlled companies capture the energy and infrastructure layer of the next era, the United States could transition from petrodollar to orbital-solar dominance. If they fail, or if Chinese manufacturing and African resource control define the supply chain, American power could erode faster than any peacetime precedent suggests. The United States, uniquely among nations, has the capacity to be the architect of the next system or its primary casualty. It cannot be both, and at the moment it appears to be trying.

V. The Variable That Changes Everything

I have left Africa until this point deliberately, because if the preceding sections describe a transition that is plausible, the African dimension is what makes it consequential at a civilisational scale.

The Sahara Desert receives more solar energy annually than the entire world currently consumes. That sentence is worth reading twice. The continent has the highest average solar irradiation of any landmass on Earth. East Africa's Great Rift Valley provides geothermal resources among the most accessible anywhere. The Congo Basin's river systems hold enormous untapped hydroelectric potential.

And then the minerals. The Democratic Republic of Congo contains over 70 percent of global cobalt reserves. Zimbabwe, Mali, and others hold significant lithium. South Africa dominates platinum group metals. Manganese, rare earths, and graphite are distributed across the continent in quantities that make Africa's mineral endowment structurally indispensable to the energy transition regardless of which technologies ultimately win.

By the metrics of the petrodollar era, Africa is peripheral. By the metrics of the energy era, it is potentially the most important landmass on Earth.

The historical pattern, of course, has been extraction: African nations supply raw inputs to supply chains whose value is captured elsewhere. Cobalt mined in Congo under appalling conditions enters a value chain whose profits accumulate in battery companies and consumer electronics firms an ocean away. The question is whether that pattern repeats or breaks. The minerals have not been depleted. The solar infrastructure has not been built. The value chain has not been locked in. But the window is not permanent, and the actors who benefit from the current arrangement have long experience in maintaining it.

The African Continental Free Trade Area, operational since 2021, is the largest free trade zone in the world by number of participating nations. A unified African energy and mineral bloc (Saharan solar, East African geothermal, Central African minerals, West African industrial capacity) would be a genuinely formidable economic entity. The African Union collectively represents 1.4 billion people and the fastest-growing demographic on Earth. Whether its leadership recognises and acts on this window, or whether fragmentation continues to serve external interests as it has since colonial partition, is one of the most consequential questions of the next fifty years.

The distribution of sunlight and minerals does not respect the hierarchy that oil and finance created. The nations that are poor by the metrics of the financial era may be sitting on the physical wealth of the energy era, and not know it yet.

VI. How Energy Actually Becomes Money

This is the section where the thesis either stands or falls, and I want to treat it with the seriousness it demands. The objection is straightforward: even if everything above is correct, how does energy actually become a unit of account? It is the right question. And the answer, I think, is more robust than sceptics expect.

It Is an Old Idea Whose Time May Have Come

Frederick Soddy, a Nobel Prize-winning chemist, argued in 1926 that real wealth derives from using energy to transform materials into useful goods, and that money is a virtual abstraction sitting on top of that physical reality. The Technocracy movement of the 1930s formally proposed replacing monetary units with energy certificates. Nicholas Georgescu-Roegen formalised the connection between thermodynamics and economics in the 1970s. Daniel Goldman's 2023 work on exergy theory of value formalised the intuition that economic value maps to usable energy. There are functioning cryptocurrencies today that tie token issuance to verified energy production.

This is not a novelty idea someone had on a podcast. It is a century-old theoretical tradition that has been waiting, essentially, for the physical infrastructure to catch up with the theory. That infrastructure is now being built.

Transitions Are Never Agreed Upon

Here is the thing most people miss about monetary transitions, and it is the thing that makes the timing so difficult to predict: they have never, in the entire historical record, been managed. They have never been planned. They have never been the product of international agreement.

What happens is simpler and more brutal. The existing system becomes unable to accommodate the new economic reality. A functional alternative exists. Pressure builds. And then, usually in a crisis, the old architecture gives way.

The petrodollar's logic requires two conditions: oil must remain the dominant scarce energy input, and the United States must maintain the military capacity and political will to enforce the Gulf pricing arrangement. Both are eroding. As AI and robotics reduce labour inputs toward zero, energy becomes the binding constraint on production: running the AI, powering the robots, processing the minerals, cooling the data centres. The entity that produces energy most cheaply gains structural advantage regardless of what governments prefer to write on their banknotes.

The transition will follow the pattern it has always followed. Gradual, then sudden. Slow erosion of dollar-denominated oil trade as more transactions occur in yuan, in rupees, in bilateral swap arrangements. Then a rapid restructuring once critical mass is reached. This is approximately what happened in 1971: years of building pressure, then a decision in an afternoon that changed the world permanently.

The Strongest Objections

I want to steelman these rather than dismiss them, because the strongest version of this thesis is the one that has engaged honestly with the best counterarguments.

Fungibility: A kilowatt hour of solar in the Sahara is not identical to a kilowatt hour of wind in Scotland. True. But the dollar already abstracts away far more heterogeneous underlying realities. A standardised energy commodity market denominating kilowatt hour equivalents across sources and locations is technically and institutionally achievable. Electricity futures markets exist today and are expanding. The infrastructure for pricing and trading energy as a commodity is not hypothetical. It is operational.

Storability: Energy dissipates while money can be saved. Partly addressed by battery technology, partly by a deeper shift. If energy is genuinely abundant and cheap on demand, the need to hoard it as a hedge against scarcity diminishes. The function of money as a store of value matters most in a world of scarcity. In a world of energy abundance, the ability to generate cheaply on demand may matter more than the ability to stockpile.

Government resistance: This is, counterintuitively, the weakest objection. Not because governments lack the desire to maintain monetary sovereignty, but because historically they have always lacked the ability to prevent transitions that the underlying economics demanded. They did not voluntarily leave the gold standard. They were forced by war. They did not voluntarily end Bretton Woods. They were forced by overspending. No government in history has successfully maintained a monetary architecture whose physical basis had collapsed. I see no reason to believe the next transition will be different in this respect.

This is where the thesis is weakest: timing. I want to be honest about this. Everything above could be directionally correct and still unfold over forty years rather than fifteen. The cost curves are real. The infrastructure buildout is real. The geopolitical repositioning is real. But the speed at which these translate into a new monetary architecture is genuinely uncertain. Anyone who tells you they know the timing is either lying or selling something. What I will say is that the historical pattern, for what it is worth, suggests these things happen faster than consensus expects. Every previous monetary transition looked slow until the moment it was sudden.

VII. What This Means for You, Right Now

A thesis about a multi-decade transition still carries immediate practical implications, because markets do not price the present. They price the anticipated future. And some of those prices are already moving.

January 2026 US inflation came in at 2.4 percent year-over-year, an eight-month low. Energy prices fell 1.5 percent month-on-month. AI-driven logistics and predictive analytics are trimming 5 to 12 percent off manufacturing and retail costs. These deflationary signals are appearing during significant fiscal expansion and tariff pressure. The coherent explanation is that structural technology-driven deflation is already offsetting what would otherwise be inflationary forces. If that is correct, we are watching the early empirical signature of the transition this essay describes.

The US-China trade conflict, framed publicly as a dispute over intellectual property and trade imbalances, is better understood as a competition over the physical infrastructure of the energy transition: chips, rare earths, solar supply chains, battery chemistry, orbital launch capability. The tariffs are not primarily economic instruments. They are strategic moves in a contest over who controls the productive base of the next monetary era.

If You Run a Country

Nations with solar and mineral resources face a choice that does not stay open indefinitely. Australia's combination of solar irradiation and mineral endowments gives it a structural advantage it has consistently failed to industrialise around. The Norwegian model (sovereign wealth, domestic value-add, long-term reinvestment) is the clearest template. African nations collectively face the most consequential version of this choice: industrialise the value chain or watch the extraction pattern repeat under a new name.

If You Run a Career

This is more immediate than the investment question. If energy and raw materials are the scarce inputs of the coming economy, roles that add value to those inputs (energy systems, materials science, robotics, AI development, the infrastructure connecting them) are structurally positioned. Roles whose primary value is cognitive labour that AI can replicate are exposed to the same pressure that automation brought to manufacturing. This is not a judgment about the worth of that work. It is an observation about the direction of the cost curves.

The investment implication is simpler and less dependent on the full thesis being precisely correct: the directional trends in solar costs, AI capability, and robotics are empirically real regardless of whether they produce an energy-denominated currency in any specific timeframe. Assets exposed to those trends carry structural tailwinds that do not require the complete thesis to materialise in order to reward early positioning.

VIII. What the Pattern Teaches

The thesis, stripped to its core, is this: currency has always reflected the dominant scarce resource of economic production. Land gave way to gold. Gold gave way to oil. Oil, as the binding energy input to an industrial economy run on human labour, gave rise to the petrodollar.

AI is making cognitive labour free. Robotics is making physical labour free. Solar is making energy nearly free. When those three processes reach their conclusions, the remaining scarce inputs are raw materials and energy. The entity that produces the most energy at the lowest cost, controls the critical mineral supply chains, and deploys autonomous intelligence at scale becomes the institution whose productive capacity underlies the system everyone else participates in. That is what a central bank was. That is what the next version of it will be.

The transition will not be announced. It will not be negotiated at a summit. It will happen the way every previous transition happened: under pressure, unevenly, faster than expected, and in ways that reward those who positioned early.

But I want to end on something the pattern also teaches, which is less often noted. Every monetary transition has eventually been absorbed. The gold standard collapsed, and the world did not end. Bretton Woods collapsed, and the world did not end. The petrodollar will erode, and the world will not end. What changes is who holds power, who creates value, and on what physical foundation the next era of human civilisation is built. These are not small questions. But they are, in the sweep of history, survivable ones.

The question is not whether this transition occurs. It is whether you see it coming, and what you do with the time between now and the moment it becomes obvious to everyone.

We have gone from gold, to a dollar backed by gold, to a dollar backed by oil. The logic of the transition points to what comes next: value backed by energy itself. The only uncertainty is timing. And timing, as history consistently demonstrates, favours those who moved before the consensus caught up.
