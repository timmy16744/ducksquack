---
title: "The Refinery That Closed"
date: "2022-08-15"
color: "orange"
---

The ExxonMobil refinery at Altona closed last year. I want to trace what happened next, because the closure itself was a headline and what followed was not, and what followed matters more.

A refinery is not a building. It is a node in a network. Feedstock comes in, crude oil, natural gas liquids, intermediary chemicals. Products go out, fuel, plastics precursors, chemical inputs for downstream manufacturers. Around the refinery, an ecosystem exists: companies that supply it, companies that depend on its output, companies that service its equipment, workers who maintain specialised knowledge about its operations. The refinery is the visible structure. The ecosystem is the thing that actually matters.

When Altona closed, the node was removed. The network did not simply reroute. It degraded.

Downstream manufacturers that relied on Altona for feedstock had to source alternatives from overseas. This sounds simple in a trade policy document. In practice, it means longer lead times, higher transport costs, different material specifications that require retesting and recertification, and the loss of the close relationship between supplier and customer that allows rapid problem-solving when something goes wrong in production. A phone call to a plant twenty kilometres away becomes an email to a facility in Singapore, operating in a different time zone, with different priorities, subject to different disruptions.

The workers dispersed. Refinery operations require specialised knowledge, process engineering, safety systems, the particular understanding of how a specific facility behaves under specific conditions. This knowledge is not generic. A process engineer from Altona cannot simply walk into a different refinery and perform at the same level, because every facility has its own characteristics, its own history, its own accumulated workarounds and institutional knowledge. When the workers leave, that knowledge leaves with them. Some will find work in related industries. Some will retrain. Some will retire. None of them will pass the specific knowledge of Altona's operations to a successor, because there is no successor.

Australia once had seven refineries. We now have two. The decline has been treated, in each instance, as an isolated commercial decision. ExxonMobil determined that Altona was no longer economically viable. Shell made a similar determination about Clyde. BP about Bulwer Island and Kwinana. Each closure was announced, reported, protested briefly, and then absorbed into the general background of economic change.

The cumulative effect has not been absorbed. It has been ignored.

We now import the majority of our refined fuel from refineries in Singapore, South Korea, and Japan. Our strategic fuel reserve, the amount of fuel available in the country at any given time, is measured in weeks. Not months. Weeks. The International Energy Agency recommends ninety days of reserve supply. Australia holds approximately twenty-five days' worth, some of it in transit on tankers that are, at this moment, somewhere on the ocean between Asian refineries and Australian ports.

If the supply were interrupted, by a conflict in the South China Sea, by a natural disaster that damaged key port infrastructure, by a geopolitical event that disrupted shipping through the straits that our imports traverse, Australia would begin running out of fuel in less than a month. Not motor fuel only, though that would be the most visible impact. Aviation fuel. Diesel for trucks, trains, agricultural equipment. The fuel that powers the generators that back up the hospitals when the grid fails. The fuel that moves the food from the farms to the cities.

The vulnerability is not hypothetical. It is the actual, present-tense strategic position of a country that is entirely surrounded by water and depends on maritime trade for its survival.

Each refinery closure was a commercial decision. Each one was rational in isolation. The company was losing money. The economics did not work. The alternative, continued operation at a loss, or government subsidy to maintain a capability the market could not sustain, was unappealing to shareholders and to treasurers.

But the accumulation of individually rational commercial decisions has produced a nationally irrational strategic outcome. We have optimised for commercial efficiency and achieved strategic vulnerability. The market did its job. The job the market did left us unable to fuel our own economy for more than a few weeks without foreign supply.

The pattern repeats across the industrial base. Each closure treated as a one-off. Each one lamented briefly and forgotten. The tooling shipped offshore. The institutional knowledge dispersed. The capability lost. And nobody, no institution, no minister, no strategic framework, is tracking the cumulative loss or calculating the point at which the accumulation becomes critical.

A refinery is not just a facility. It is a capability. Capabilities, once lost, do not return on request. They take decades to rebuild, if they can be rebuilt at all. The supply chains must be reestablished. The workforce must be retrained. The institutional knowledge must be redeveloped from scratch. The cost of rebuilding a capability is always greater than the cost of maintaining it, and the time required is always longer than the crisis allows.

We are dismantling our industrial capacity one commercial decision at a time. Each decision is rational. The trajectory they produce is not.
