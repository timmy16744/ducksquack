---
title: "What Survived the Crash"
date: "2023-06-15"
color: "orange"
---

FTX collapsed in November. Sam Bankman-Fried, who had been on the cover of magazines and testifying before Congress and donating millions to political campaigns, turned out to be running something between a Ponzi scheme and a casino with customer deposits. Eight billion dollars in customer funds, missing. The second-largest cryptocurrency exchange in the world, vapourised in a week.

Before FTX, Terra/Luna had imploded, a so-called stablecoin that proved to be neither stable nor coined, whose algorithmic peg to the US dollar unwound in a death spiral that destroyed forty billion dollars of notional value in seventy-two hours. Before that, Three Arrows Capital, a hedge fund that had leveraged itself into oblivion. Before that, a parade of smaller collapses, rug pulls, and outright frauds that had been the background noise of the crypto industry for years but that the bull market had made easy to ignore.

The hype has collapsed. The fraud has been exposed. The influencers who promoted projects they did not understand to audiences that trusted them have moved on to promoting other things. The industry, as it existed in 2021, the Bored Apes, the yield farming, the DeFi protocols promising implausible returns, the entire superstructure of speculation and marketing and manufactured urgency, is largely dead, and not many people outside the industry mourn it.

Good. Most of it deserved to die.

And yet.

Bitcoin is still here. The network runs. The blockchain has not been hacked, not in fifteen years of operation, not once. The protocol continues to produce blocks every ten minutes, executing transactions, maintaining consensus across a distributed network of nodes that no single entity controls. The price has crashed, from $69,000 to roughly $25,000 as I write this, but the system functions exactly as it always has. The crash was in the price. Not in the infrastructure.

This distinction matters, and I think it gets lost in the justified satisfaction of watching fraudsters exposed.

The fraud was real. The speculation was absurd. The industry attracted a concentration of charlatans, grifters, and incompetents that was impressive even by the standards of financial manias. The regulatory vacuum was exploited by people whose primary skill was the appearance of legitimacy, and the harm they caused, to retail investors who lost savings, to institutions that lost credibility, to the concept of digital finance itself, was substantial and in many cases unforgivable.

But the underlying demand signal is genuine. Millions of people, across dozens of countries, decided they wanted monetary infrastructure that no government could debase or seize. This desire did not originate with Bitcoin and will not disappear with the crypto winter. It is a response to something real: the observation, supported by decades of evidence, that governments debase currencies, that central banks serve institutional interests that do not always align with the interests of ordinary people, that the monetary system as currently constituted transfers wealth from savers to borrowers and from the young to the old in ways that are never subjected to democratic approval.

I wrote two years ago about the money printer and the emperor. The parallel between modern quantitative easing and Roman currency debasement. The mechanism by which newly created money flows to asset prices, enriching owners and impoverishing workers. The hidden tax that nobody voted for.

Bitcoin, specifically Bitcoin, not the broader crypto industry, which is largely noise, is a response to that mechanism. An attempt, however imperfect, to create money that cannot be printed. That has a fixed supply. That is governed by mathematics rather than institutions. That is resistant to the specific form of manipulation that has characterised monetary systems for as long as monetary systems have existed.

This does not mean Bitcoin is the answer. It may not be. The energy consumption is problematic. The volatility makes it impractical as a medium of exchange. The concentration of holdings among early adopters creates its own form of inequality. The technology has limitations that may prove fundamental rather than solvable.

But the search for alternatives to institutional money is not going away. The demand for monetary systems that cannot be debased, that are transparent in their operations, that do not require trust in institutions that have repeatedly proven untrustworthy, this demand is a feature of the current moment, not a fad that the crypto winter will extinguish.

From Australia, the monetary landscape has a specific character. Our entire national wealth is denominated in a housing market priced in Australian dollars, managed by a Reserve Bank that follows the lead of the Federal Reserve, subject to the same inflationary pressures and the same debasement dynamics that characterise all fiat currencies. Our monetary sovereignty is as thin as our manufacturing sovereignty. We do not set the price of money. We follow those who do.

The hype collapsed. The fraud was exposed. The underlying demand for monetary alternatives did not disappear. It went quiet, which is different from going away.

The question is not whether people will keep searching for alternatives to institutional money. They will, because the conditions that drive the search, debasement, declining trust, the distributional unfairness of the current system, are intensifying, not resolving.

The question is what they will find.
