---
title: "The Burning Library"
date: "2020-10-10"
color: "orange"
---

A man I met last year, at a manufacturing conference I attended out of curiosity rather than professional obligation, told me he was one of the last people in Australia who could operate a specific type of extrusion machine. He was fifty-seven. His company had closed two years earlier. The machine, which had been in operation since the 1980s, had been scrapped. Nobody had recorded how to run it, because nobody imagined that the recording would be necessary. The knowledge was in his hands and his head, and when he retired it would cease to exist.

I have been thinking about this man, and others like him, since the pandemic began accelerating closures across the manufacturing and industrial sectors. Not because the closures are surprising, the decline has been underway for decades, but because the pandemic has compressed what might have been a gradual process into a rapid one, and the speed matters.

When a factory closes slowly, there is at least the possibility of knowledge transfer. A veteran worker might train a replacement. A company might document its processes. The institutional memory might find a new home, even if degraded. When a factory closes suddenly, when a pandemic disrupts supply chains, when government support runs out, when the economics that were already marginal become definitively unviable, the knowledge simply disappears.

I want to argue that this is the least discussed and most consequential aspect of industrial decline. Not the jobs, though the jobs matter. Not the economic output, though the output matters. The knowledge. The accumulated, embodied, distributed understanding of how to make things, which exists in no textbook and no database but only in the networks of people who have done it, together, for years.

Every factory is a library. Not in the metaphorical, inspirational-poster sense. In the literal sense that it contains knowledge that exists nowhere else. The machinist who can feel when a cutting tool is about to fail, from the vibration transmitted through the workpiece, is carrying information that no sensor has been built to capture. The process engineer who knows that a particular polymer behaves differently on humid days is holding a data point that appears in no spec sheet. The production manager who understands which suppliers can actually deliver and which will promise and fail is maintaining a network map that no CRM system contains.

This knowledge is tacit. It resists codification. It is acquired through years of practice, refined through thousands of small decisions, embedded in the muscle memory and the intuitive judgment of people who have been doing the work long enough to know what the work actually requires, as opposed to what the manual says it requires.

When those people leave, when the factory closes, when the industry contracts, the knowledge goes with them. It does not sit in a recoverable archive. It disperses into the general population, where it degrades rapidly through lack of use, and within a generation it is gone entirely.

Australia has been burning its industrial libraries for decades. The auto industry took a hundred and twenty supplier companies with it when it closed in 2017. Each one of those companies was a library. The textile industry, which contracted through the 1980s and 1990s, was a library. The refineries that have closed, the steel mills that have shrunk, the chemical plants that have shut down, each one a library, burning.

The pandemic has accelerated the burning. Companies that were marginal before COVID have become unviable during it. The support packages keep some alive temporarily, but the trajectory is clear. When the support ends, the closures will resume, and each closure will take its library with it.

What concerns me is not that this is happening, industrial change has always involved the loss of some knowledge, but that we have no framework for even measuring what we are losing. We track job losses. We track economic output. We track trade balances and GDP contributions. We do not track knowledge loss, because we do not have a metric for it, because tacit knowledge is by definition the kind of thing that resists measurement.

The replacement myth, that displaced workers can be reskilled for the new economy, depends on an assumption that the knowledge lost was generic and transferable. It was not. A fifty-seven-year-old extrusion specialist cannot become a data scientist. Not because he lacks intelligence but because the knowledge he holds is specific, embodied, and non-transferable. It is a different kind of knowing, and the economy does not currently value it, which does not mean it is not valuable. It means we have not yet discovered the cost of losing it.

That cost will emerge. It always does. When we need to build something and discover that nobody knows how. When a supply chain fails and there is no domestic alternative because the people who could have provided one are retired or dead or driving Ubers. When a crisis demands manufacturing capacity and the capacity does not exist and cannot be recreated on the timeline the crisis requires.

Civilisations decline not when they lose wealth but when they lose knowledge. The wealth can be regenerated. The knowledge, once lost, requires generations to rebuild, if it can be rebuilt at all.

The libraries are burning. We are not even cataloguing what is being lost.
