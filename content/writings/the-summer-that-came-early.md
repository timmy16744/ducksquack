---
title: "The Summer That Came Early"
date: "2019-11-20"
color: "red"
---

The fire season started in September. This is not normal. Fire season in southeastern Australia begins, in the pattern most of us grew up with, in December or January. The heat builds, the land dries, the eucalyptus oil hangs in the air like a promise, and eventually a lightning strike or a careless match sets it off. You learn the rhythm as a child. You learn that summer has a dangerous edge, that the beauty of the Australian landscape is inseparable from its capacity for destruction.

September is spring. September is supposed to be mild, green, the pause between winter rain and summer heat. In September 2019, parts of New South Wales and Queensland were already burning.

By November, when I write this, the fires have been burning for weeks and show no sign of stopping. Sydney, not the bush, not the rural fringe, but Sydney, a city of five million people, has been blanketed in smoke so thick that the air quality index exceeded hazardous levels for days at a time. People wore masks to work. Schools kept children indoors. The sky turned orange in the middle of the day, not in the distant, romantic way that bushfire sunsets sometimes colour the horizon, but in the immediate, oppressive way that tells you the fire is not somewhere else. It is here. In the air you are breathing. In the light that falls on your face.

I am in Adelaide, where the smoke has arrived in drifts, less intense than Sydney but present. You can smell it. That particular Australian smell, eucalyptus ash, that your body associates with danger even before your mind has caught up.

I want to write about what this feels like, because I think the feeling matters more than the statistics, though the statistics are alarming enough.

It feels like the models have come alive. For years, climate science has produced projections. Graphs with upward-trending lines. Scenarios labelled with bureaucratic precision: RCP 4.5, RCP 8.5. Conference presentations with cautious language about probabilities and confidence intervals. The projections were always for the future, always slightly abstract, always mediated by the professional caution of scientists who know the cost of overstating their case.

The projections are no longer for the future. They are for now. The fire season that climate models projected for 2040 or 2050 has arrived in 2019. Not as a one-off anomaly that can be attributed to natural variation. As a pattern. Hotter, drier, earlier, worse. Consistent with the models. Earlier than the median projections suggested. But within the range. Always within the range, if you bothered to read the range instead of the median.

This is what it feels like when the models stop being projections and start being descriptions. It feels like something has shifted, not in the physical world, which has been shifting gradually for decades, but in the relationship between the world and your expectations of it. The assumption that this year would be roughly like last year, that the future would be an extension of the past, that the rhythms you grew up with would persist, this assumption has broken. Not in theory. In the air.

Australia is the driest inhabited continent. It is the most fire-prone. The vegetation has evolved to burn, which is a remarkable adaptation in evolutionary terms and a terrifying feature of the landscape you live in. Eucalyptus trees are essentially standing fuel, their oil-rich leaves designed by millions of years of selection to ignite, to burn fast and hot, to clear the understory and release seeds in the process. The landscape is built to burn. Human settlement within that landscape depends on the fires being manageable. On the season being predictable. On the patterns holding.

The patterns are not holding.

What strikes me most, watching the political response, is the gap between the scale of the problem and the scale of the conversation about it. The Prime Minister goes on holiday to Hawaii while the fires burn. This is not, I think, merely bad optics. It is a genuine failure of comprehension. A failure to understand that the fires are not an event to be managed but a signal to be read. That the summer came early because the system that produces summers has changed, and the change is not temporary, and the political vocabulary of response, emergency funding, deployment of resources, thoughts and prayers, is inadequate to the nature of what is happening.

We are a continent governed by people who think in electoral cycles confronting a problem that operates on geological timescales. The mismatch is not one of policy. It is one of categories. The political system is designed to address problems that have solutions within the timeline of a parliamentary term. Climate change does not have solutions within the timeline of a parliamentary term. It has responses, adaptations, investments in resilience that take decades to mature. The political system cannot see in decades. It can barely see past the next Newspoll.

I am not a climate scientist and I cannot say with certainty what this means. But I pay attention to patterns, and the pattern I observe is a gap, between the world that is emerging and the institutions that are supposed to manage it, that is growing wider, and the fires are the most visible manifestation of that gap, but not the most important.

The most important manifestation is the one you cannot see from the window. It is the slow, compounding failure of a civilisation to update its assumptions in the face of evidence that the assumptions are wrong. We plan for the climate we grew up in. We build for the weather we remember. We govern as if the future will resemble the past.

The summer came early. The question is whether we are capable of updating, or whether we will keep planning for a world that no longer exists while the smoke from the one we actually inhabit drifts through our cities.
