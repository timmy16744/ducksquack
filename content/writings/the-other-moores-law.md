---
title: "The Other Moore's Law"
date: "2023-09-15"
color: "red"
---

Something is happening in Chinese artificial intelligence that most Western observers have not fully registered. I want to describe what I see, as clearly as I can, without the alarmism that usually accompanies this subject and without the complacency that usually follows the alarmism.

In August, a Chinese company called Baidu released Ernie Bot, a large language model that performs, by most benchmarks, within striking distance of GPT-4. This is notable not because of Ernie Bot specifically but because of what it represents: the closing of a gap that, eighteen months ago, appeared to be widening.

When ChatGPT launched in late 2022, the dominant assumption in Western technology circles was that American AI was so far ahead that meaningful Chinese competition was years away. The reasoning was superficial but compelling: the United States had the best researchers, the most advanced chip design, the deepest capital markets, and the cultural environment, open, competitive, intellectually free, that innovation requires.

The reasoning was wrong. Not entirely wrong. But wrong enough to matter.

Let me trace the structural position, because the structure is what determines trajectory, and trajectory is what matters in a technology race.

Manufacturing. China produces the majority of the world's electronics. Not designs them, the design talent is distributed, but physically manufactures them. The fabs, the assembly lines, the supply chains that transform designs into products exist overwhelmingly in China and Taiwan. This manufacturing base provides something that no amount of research talent can substitute: the ability to build at scale, rapidly, and iterate physically rather than theoretically.

Energy. China has invested more in energy infrastructure in the last decade than any nation in history. Solar, wind, nuclear, coal, the mix is not clean, but the scale is extraordinary. AI training requires enormous amounts of compute, and compute requires enormous amounts of energy. China is building the energy capacity to power the next generation of AI development in a way that Western nations, constrained by planning processes and community opposition, are not.

State coordination. The Chinese system eliminates the separation between civilian innovation and strategic application that characterises Western approaches. In the United States, technology transfer from Silicon Valley to the Pentagon is slow, bureaucratic, and frequently adversarial. In China, civil-military fusion is not a slogan but an organising principle. A breakthrough in commercial AI becomes a military capability within months, not years.

Data. China has access to data at a scale and with a degree of permissiveness that Western democracies cannot match. Privacy regulations constrain what Western companies can collect and how they can use it. China's regulatory environment, while not without constraints, operates with fundamentally different assumptions about the balance between individual privacy and state access. More data, used with fewer restrictions, produces better models. The observation is technical, not moral.

Open source. This is the development I find most strategically significant. Rather than competing with American closed-source models on their terms, Chinese AI development has embraced open source as a strategic posture. If you cannot control the frontier, democratise it. Make the baseline free and compete on integration, deployment, and scale. The open-source models emerging from Chinese labs, and from Chinese-funded research globally, are narrowing the gap between what is freely available and what sits behind American corporate walls.

I want to take the counterarguments seriously, because they have merit.

Western innovation culture remains vibrant. The best researchers still disproportionately choose American institutions. The quality of Chinese AI research, while improving rapidly, may still lag in ways that benchmarks do not capture, in the kind of creative, paradigm-shifting work that produces genuine breakthroughs rather than incremental improvements. The institutional trust that enables scientific collaboration is stronger in open societies. These points are real.

I am not certain they are sufficient.

The manufacturing base exists. The energy infrastructure exists. The state coordination exists. The open-source ecosystem is expanding. These are not future possibilities. They are present realities.

For Australia, the implications are acute. We are caught between our security alliance with the United States and our economic dependence on China. We have no independent AI capability of significance. We have no chip fabrication. We have no manufacturing base that could support the physical infrastructure of AI development. Our choices are whose AI infrastructure to depend on, not whether to depend.

The assumption that we will always have access to frontier AI capabilities through our alliance relationships, that American technology will be shared with Australian allies as a matter of course, may prove optimistic. Technology sharing follows strategic interests, and strategic interests shift. If Chinese AI capabilities match or exceed Western capabilities, the strategic calculus changes in ways that are difficult to predict and may not favour small allies.

History is full of moments when one civilisation's assumptions about its own technological superiority proved unfounded. I do not know if this is such a moment. I think the question deserves more attention than it currently receives in a country that has chosen, whether deliberately or by default, to be a spectator in the most consequential technology race in human history.
