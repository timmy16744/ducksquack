---
title: "What the Model Sees"
date: "2023-08-15"
color: "blue"
---

A machine generated a photograph this week. Not a photograph in the traditional sense, no light passed through a lens, no sensor captured a scene, but something that is, to the human eye, indistinguishable from a photograph. A face. A specific face, with specific features, specific lighting, specific imperfections. A face that has never existed, depicting a person who has never lived, rendered with a fidelity that makes detection, even by trained observers, unreliable.

This is new. Not in the way that most technology is new, where the capability is an incremental extension of what came before. New in the sense that a boundary has been crossed. The boundary between what is real and what is generated. Between what happened and what could have happened. Between the captured and the fabricated.

The implications of this are difficult to overstate, and I want to trace them carefully because I think they extend further than the immediate and obvious concerns about deepfakes and misinformation.

Start with the obvious. If a machine can generate a convincing image of something that never happened, the evidentiary value of images has collapsed. A photograph used to be a form of proof. Not perfect proof, photographs have been manipulated since the medium was invented, but proof in the general sense that the existence of a photograph created a strong presumption that the scene it depicted had occurred. Courts admitted photographs as evidence. Journalism relied on photographs as documentation. Personal photographs served as proof of memory, you were here, this happened, this is what it looked like.

That presumption is over. Any image can now be fabricated, by anyone, at no cost, with a level of quality that makes detection unreliable. A photograph of a politician at a meeting that never occurred. A photograph of a building that never existed. A photograph of your face doing something you never did. The technology does not require expertise. It requires a prompt.

The extension to video is already underway. The quality is lower, the artefacts more visible, but the trajectory is clear. Within a year or two, perhaps less, the fabrication of convincing video will be as accessible as the fabrication of convincing images is today. At that point, the evidentiary value of video, the medium that currently serves as our most trusted form of documentation, will follow the same path as photography. From proof to suggestion. From evidence to ambiguity.

The problem extends beyond the technical into something more fundamental: an epistemic transformation. We have, for roughly a century and a half, relied on recorded media as a bridge between experience and knowledge. I was not at the event, but I saw the photograph. I was not in the room, but I watched the video. The media served as a proxy for presence, extending the reach of individual experience far beyond the limits of direct observation.

That bridge is becoming unreliable. And the consequences of an unreliable bridge are not the same as the consequences of no bridge at all. When there was no photography, people relied on testimony, on written accounts, on the judgments of observers whose credibility was individually assessed. The absence of recorded media did not prevent knowledge; it simply channelled it through different means.

The unreliability of recorded media is worse than its absence. It creates a world in which any recorded evidence can be both presented and denied. Any photograph can be claimed as real or dismissed as fabricated. Any video can be offered as proof or rejected as generated. The result is not a world without evidence but a world where evidence has lost its ability to settle disputes, and the settlement of disputes through shared evidence is one of the foundations on which democratic society rests.

But I want to go deeper than the epistemic concern, because there is something more personal here that I find harder to articulate.

I think about memory. My own memory, which is unreliable in the way that all human memory is unreliable, reconstructive rather than reproductive, editing itself with each retrieval, filling gaps with plausible fabrication. I have photographs from my travels. Forty-five countries, hundreds of images. When I look at them, they serve a specific function: they anchor memory. They provide a fixed point against which the drift of recollection can be checked. I was here. This is what it looked like. The photograph is proof against the natural erosion of recall.

If photographs can be fabricated, this anchoring function weakens. Not because my personal photographs are fabricated, but because the existence of fabrication as a possibility introduces doubt into the relationship between image and memory. The photograph used to be a window into the past. It is becoming a mirror that might show the past or might show something else, and the distinction between the two is no longer reliable.

There is something deeper still. If a machine can generate convincing representations of things that never happened, then the concept of documentation itself begins to shift. What is a record? What is an archive? What does it mean to say that something happened, when the evidence for and against can be fabricated with equal ease?

These questions may sound philosophical. They are not. They are practical questions that will affect how courts function, how journalism operates, how history is written, how institutions maintain accountability, how individuals relate to their own pasts. The answers to these questions will shape the texture of daily life in ways that are difficult to predict from this side of the transformation.

I find myself in a strange position. I am someone who values evidence, who built a career on the ability to see clearly and design accordingly, who has written extensively about the importance of honest assessment. And I am watching the infrastructure of honest assessment, the media through which evidence is transmitted, become fundamentally unreliable.

The models see everything and nothing. They can generate any image but they cannot attest to any truth. They are mirrors that reflect whatever prompt they are given, with no commitment to what actually occurred. The gap between what the model sees and what is real is not a flaw to be corrected. It is a feature of the architecture. The model does not know what is real. It knows what is probable. And probability, it turns out, is not the same as truth.

We built these tools to generate content. We may have accidentally built tools that dissolve the boundary between what happened and what could have happened. The consequences of that dissolution are not yet clear. But they will be. And by the time they are clear, the boundary will already be gone.
