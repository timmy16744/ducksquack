title: "Controlled Substances"
date: "2020-11-15"

Melbourne is in its sixth month of lockdown. The longest in the world. I am in Adelaide, where the restrictions have been less severe but the atmosphere is the same: a pervasive, low-grade sense of being managed. Told where you can go. Told who you can see. Told what constitutes an essential reason to leave your house.

I comply. This is worth stating. I wear the mask. I check in. I follow the rules. I write this from a position of recognition, not defiance. I recognise this energy. It has happened before.

In the late 1960s and early 1970s, an entire generation looked at the machinery of the state, the draft that sent young men to die in Vietnam, the surveillance that monitored domestic dissent, the censorship that controlled what could be said and shown and thought, and experienced a collective realisation: the government does not have the right to do this. The counterculture was many things, chaotic and naive and self-indulgent among them. But at its core it was a recognition that the state had claimed authority over the bodies and minds of its citizens that it had not earned and could not justify.

I feel that energy now. Not from the same direction, not wearing the same clothes, but the same essential vibration. The state has decided what you may do with your body. Where you may take it. Who you may bring it close to. The justification is public health, and the justification has merit. I am not a COVID denier. The virus is real. The hospitals are real. The dead are real.

But the mechanism is the same. The state claims authority over your physical person, and most people comply not because they have weighed the evidence and reached an independent conclusion but because compliance is the default. Because the alternative, questioning authority in the middle of a crisis, feels irresponsible, or dangerous, or simply exhausting.

I hold a principle that I suspect is less common than I would like: you should be permitted to do with your body whatever you choose, so long as you do not harm others. The foundational premise of liberal democracy, stated plainly. And yet in practice we abandon it routinely, reflexively, the moment an authority figure tells us it is necessary.

I want to talk about drugs. Specifically, I want to talk about psychedelics, and I want to be direct about this because directness is the only approach that respects the subject.

I have taken DMT. Not recreationally. Not at a party. In a setting that was deliberate, considered, and treated with the seriousness the substance demands. I will not describe the experience in full because the experience resists language in ways that make description almost dishonest. I will say that it was among the most significant perceptual events of my life. Not enjoyable in the way that word is normally used. Significant. The way a door opening onto a landscape you did not know existed is significant.

What I saw, what I experienced, since saw implies only the visual, was a way of perceiving that I had not known was available. The filters through which I normally experience reality, the assumptions, the categories, the unconscious architecture of perception, became visible as filters. I could see them. I could see that they were constructions, not fixed features of the world, and that the world behind the constructions was larger and stranger and more intricate than the constructions allowed.

I came back through the door. The door closed. But I had seen the other side, and the world I returned to looked different. Not because anything in the world had changed. Because I had seen, briefly and with overwhelming clarity, the degree to which my normal perception was managed. Curated. Filtered through a set of assumptions so deeply embedded that I had mistaken them for reality itself.

This experience made me think more carefully about why psychedelics are illegal. The official justification is public health. The substances are dangerous, addictive, harmful. This is the story. Here is the problem with the story: it is not true.

Psilocybin, the active compound in psychedelic mushrooms, has lower toxicity than aspirin. LSD has never been conclusively linked to a single death from overdose. DMT is produced endogenously by the human brain. The clinical literature on psychedelics, which is now being produced by research institutions at Johns Hopkins, Imperial College London, and elsewhere, consistently finds therapeutic benefits for depression, PTSD, addiction, and end-of-life anxiety. The substances that are classified as the most dangerous are, by the evidence, among the least harmful.

Meanwhile, alcohol kills thousands of Australians every year. Tobacco kills more. Both are legal, regulated, taxed, and sold at every corner shop. The scheduling of substances in Australia, as in most Western countries, bears no relationship to their actual harm profile. It bears a relationship to something else.

The timing of prohibition is instructive. Psychedelics were legal in the United States through the early 1960s, used in clinical research, administered in therapeutic settings. They were criminalised in 1970, under the Controlled Substances Act. The same year that the counterculture was at its peak. The same year that opposition to the Vietnam War was reaching critical mass. The same year that the political establishment faced the most sustained challenge to its authority since the labour movements of the early twentieth century.

John Ehrlichman, Nixon's domestic policy advisor, said the following in a 1994 interview: the Nixon campaign had two enemies, the antiwar left and Black people. They could not make it illegal to be against the war or to be Black. But they could associate the antiwar movement with marijuana and Black communities with heroin, criminalise both, and disrupt those communities. Did they know they were lying about the drugs? Of course they did.

The evidence is a direct quote from a senior official in the administration that created the modern drug war. The war on drugs was designed, explicitly and by the admission of the people who designed it, as a tool of political control. The substances were banned not because they were dangerous but because they were associated with people who were questioning authority.

Fifty years later, the framework persists. The scheduling remains. The criminalisation continues. In Australia, possession of psilocybin can result in imprisonment. A substance that is less toxic than the paracetamol in your medicine cabinet, that is being used in clinical trials at major universities, that is produced by mushrooms that grow wild in Australian paddocks. Prison.

The question I keep asking, and the question the lockdowns have sharpened, is: why do we trust them?

Not this government specifically. Government, as an institution. The entity that claims authority over our bodies, our minds, the substances we may ingest, the places we may go, the people we may see. The entity with an extensive, documented, undeniable history of lying. Of manipulating. Of pursuing institutional interests under the banner of public welfare.

None of this makes me an anarchist. I understand that coordination is necessary, that rules are necessary, that some authority is required for a society to function. But I argue for a much more sceptical posture toward governmental claims of authority than most citizens maintain. And I argue that the default of compliance, the reflexive trust that most people extend to government directives, is not earned. It is habitual. It is the residue of an education system and a media environment that treat authority as legitimate until proven otherwise, when the historical record suggests the reverse.

There is a technology approaching that could change this dynamic in ways I find genuinely exciting. Large language models, artificial intelligence systems capable of processing and synthesising vast quantities of information, are advancing rapidly. Within years, perhaps within a decade, these systems will be capable of something that has never existed: real-time, comprehensive, incorruptible accountability.

Imagine a system with access to all government communications, all budget allocations, all policy outcomes. A system that cannot be bribed, cannot be captured, cannot be threatened, cannot forget. A system whose sole function is to compare what governments say they are doing with what they are actually doing, and to make the comparison public, instantly, without editorial interference.

To be clear: algorithms should not make policy decisions. What I am describing is something more modest and more powerful: that AI could serve as a permanent, incorruptible auditor of the people who do make policy decisions. An alignment harness, not for the AI but for the government. A mechanism that makes dishonesty structurally impossible, or at least structurally visible.

The alignment problem in AI safety, how to ensure that intelligent systems pursue the goals we intend rather than goals of their own, has a mirror image in governance: how to ensure that powerful institutions pursue the public interest rather than their own. We have been trying to solve the governance alignment problem for centuries, with constitutions, with courts, with a free press. None of these mechanisms have been fully successful. All can be captured, corrupted, or circumvented.

An AI auditor might succeed where these mechanisms have partially failed. Not because AI is inherently trustworthy, it is not, but because an AI system designed specifically for transparency, with the right architecture and the right constraints, could provide a form of accountability that human institutions have never been able to sustain.

This requires solving the alignment problem for the AI itself, which is not trivial. A misaligned auditor with access to all government data would be extraordinarily dangerous. But the possibility exists, and in a world where governments have shown, repeatedly and across centuries, that they cannot be trusted to police themselves, the possibility of a non-human mechanism for enforcing honesty is worth taking seriously.

The lockdown taught me something I already suspected but had not fully felt. The state will control your body if you let it. It will control your mind if it can. The substances that expand perception are banned. The substances that narrow it are sold at every corner shop. This is not an accident. The question for the next decade is whether the most powerful technology ever built will be used to deepen that control or to finally, permanently, make it transparent.

I know which outcome I want. I do not know which we will get.
