title: "The Boy and the World"
date: "2023-12-20"

---

I have been thinking about fatherhood. Not in the abstract sense that people mean when they say they want children someday, the way they say they want to learn Italian or visit Patagonia, a pleasant intention that floats in the future without urgency. In the concrete sense. The sense that lands in your body and reorganises your priorities before your mind has caught up.

I want a child. I want to be a father. And I am terrified by what that means in this particular moment of history.

I.

Let me explain the terror, because it is specific and I think it is shared by more people my age than the birth rate statistics suggest.

I can see the world changing. I have spent years watching the curves, energy, AI, geopolitics, the institutional decay that I have written about extensively, and the curves tell me that the world my child would inherit will be fundamentally different from the one I grew up in. Not slightly different. Not different in the way that each generation experiences difference. Different in the way that the world before and after the printing press was different, or before and after electrification.

The skills that served me may be worthless for my child. The institutions I navigated may not exist. The career paths, the educational frameworks, the social arrangements, all of it is in flux, and the flux is accelerating.

What do you teach someone whose future you genuinely cannot predict?

The question is practical, and that every prospective parent in my generation faces, whether they articulate it or not. The previous generation could prepare their children for a world that resembled the one they knew. My parents could reasonably assume that the skills and values and strategies that worked for them would work, in modified form, for me. The assumption was not perfect, but it was functional.

I cannot make that assumption. The world that will exist when my child reaches adulthood is so uncertain that any specific preparation risks being precisely wrong. Training them for jobs that will not exist. Teaching them skills that will be automated. Preparing them for institutions that will have transformed beyond recognition.

II.

And yet.

The terror coexists with something else. Something that I find difficult to name but that feels more fundamental than the terror. A pull. A gravity. The sense that this is what I am supposed to do, not in any cosmic or teleological sense, but in the biological, emotional, deeply human sense that the desire for children is not a preference to be weighed against other preferences. It is a different category of thing. Deeper than the rational analysis I am trained to apply to everything else.

Chronic illness has shaped this in ways I am still understanding. When your body imposes limits that most people never experience, when pain structures your existence to a degree that makes planning difficult and spontaneity dangerous, you develop a relationship with time that is unusual. You know, more viscerally than most people your age, that time is finite. That the window for certain experiences does not stay open indefinitely. That the body you inhabit is not a reliable instrument and that whatever you are going to do, you should do it before the instrument fails more comprehensively than it already has.

Call it clarity. The cluster headaches have given me many things I did not ask for, and one of them is an acute awareness that life does not wait for optimal conditions. There is no perfect moment to become a parent. There is only the moment you are in, with whatever uncertainty it contains, and the decision to act within that uncertainty or to defer indefinitely and call the deferral prudence.

III.

I have travelled to forty-five countries. In many of them, I watched parents. Not deliberately, at first. But increasingly with attention, as the question of fatherhood moved from abstract to urgent.

In Japan, I watched fathers on the trains, their children immaculate in school uniforms, the interaction characterised by a quality of attentiveness that I found moving. The seriousness with which childhood was treated. The investment in the next generation that was visible not just in policy but in the daily texture of family life.

In parts of Southeast Asia, I watched children grow up in conditions that would be considered deprivation by Australian standards, and I watched them thrive, connected, engaged, resilient, embedded in extended family networks that provided a density of human contact that Australian suburban life cannot match. The material conditions were worse. The relational conditions were, in some ways, better.

In Scandinavia, I watched societies that had organised themselves around the principle that children are a collective responsibility. Parental leave that is measured in years, not weeks. Childcare that is universal and affordable. Public spaces designed for families. The assumption, embedded in policy and culture, that raising the next generation is important enough to warrant genuine societal investment.

And in Australia, I watch a society where housing costs consume so much income that starting a family requires either inherited wealth or a willingness to accept financial precariousness. Where childcare costs rival mortgage payments. Where the work culture assumes availability that is incompatible with attentive parenting. Where the birth rate declines year after year because the material conditions for raising children are deteriorating while the rhetoric about family values persists unchanged.

IV.

The question I keep returning to is not whether to become a father. That decision, I think, is made. The question is what kind of father. What do I give a child who will grow up in a world I cannot predict?

Not skills, specifically. Skills become obsolete. Not knowledge, specifically. Knowledge becomes searchable. Something else. Something harder to name and harder to teach.

Judgment. The ability to assess a situation without a manual. To read a room, a market, a moment, and know what is required without being told. This cannot be automated because it requires understanding context, and context is the thing that machines handle least well.

Resilience. Not the motivational poster version, bouncing back from setbacks. The real version, which is the ability to continue functioning under conditions of sustained uncertainty without collapsing into either denial or despair. I know something about this. The headaches have taught me, brutally and without my consent.

The capacity to find meaning in the absence of guarantees. This is what I have learned from chronic illness, from travel, from the honest examination of systems that claim to be working and are not. Meaning is not given. It is constructed. And the construction is a skill that can be practised and developed and, I believe, transmitted.

I do not know what world my child will live in. I know it will be different from mine in ways I cannot fully imagine. What I can give is not preparation for a specific future but the capacity to navigate whatever future arrives.

That, and the knowledge, which I hope they will feel before they can articulate it, that they were wanted. Not as an accessory or an obligation or a default. Wanted fiercely, deliberately, by someone who could see the storm coming and chose to bring them into it anyway. Because the storm is not the whole story. Because the world, even this world, even now, contains enough beauty and strangeness and possibility to justify the risk.

Because life, even uncertain life, even painful life, is the thing. And the desire to share it with someone new is not naivety. It is the deepest form of optimism I know.
