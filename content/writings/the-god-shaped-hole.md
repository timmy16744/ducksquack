---
title: "The God-Shaped Hole"
date: "2021-12-20"
color: "purple"
---

Two years of isolation. Two years in which the thin structures of communal life, the office, the pub, the gym, the casual encounter on the street, were suspended or restricted. Two years in which Australians, and people everywhere, were left largely alone with themselves.

And what I notice, emerging from the worst of it, is that we were already alone before the pandemic started. The lockdowns did not create the isolation. They removed the rituals that masked it. The commute. The water cooler. The Friday drinks. The small, unremarkable interactions that provided just enough social contact to prevent most people from noticing how thin the fabric had become.

The fabric was thin because something was removed from it, decades ago, and nothing was installed in its place. I want to talk about what was removed, and I want to do so from an unusual position: as someone who does not believe in the thing that was removed but who believes the removal has cost us more than we have acknowledged.

I.

I grew up attending chapel at Prince Alfred College in Adelaide. Uniting Church. Hymns on Wednesday mornings. The Lord's Prayer before meals. The architecture of worship, stone and timber and stained glass, surrounding me daily for years.

I did not believe. Not as a child, not as a teenager, not now. I sat in the pews and found the hymns beautiful and the sermons sometimes interesting and the rituals strangely moving, and at no point did I believe that there was a God listening to the prayers or a Christ who had risen from the dead or a cosmic plan that justified the suffering I could already see in the world around me.

I call myself agnostic, and I mean it seriously. Not as a polite way of saying atheist. As an intellectual commitment. I do not have sufficient information to determine whether there is a consciousness behind the architecture of the universe. The question strikes me as genuinely open, the strangeness of existence, the fine-tuning of physical constants, the hard problem of consciousness, these are not settled matters that point clearly in either direction. Certainty, in either direction, seems to me a failure of humility.

What I do not believe is that any specific religion has access to truths about the nature of the divine that other religions lack. I find wisdom in Christianity, the emphasis on compassion, on forgiveness, on the dignity of every person. I find wisdom in Hinduism, the understanding that the self is not as fixed as it appears, that reality has layers, that the cycle of existence has a depth that materialist frameworks cannot fully capture. I take these insights where I find them and do not worry much about the metaphysical packaging.

But this essay is not about what I believe. It is about what was lost when belief receded, and whether the loss has been adequately reckoned with.

II.

Religion, whatever else it was, provided infrastructure. Not just buildings, though the buildings mattered. Infrastructure for human needs that do not disappear when the metaphysical framework is removed.

Community. A weekly practice of sitting in a room with your neighbours and contemplating something beyond the immediate. Something other than a networking event or a gym class or a meeting with an agenda. A gathering whose purpose was presence itself, the act of being together in the same space, oriented toward something larger than individual concerns. The secular world has no equivalent. We have tried, community centres, clubs, co-working spaces, online forums, and none of them replicate the specific quality of regular, local, purpose-driven gathering that religious communities provided.

Ritual. The marking of birth and death, of marriage and loss, of the transitions that structure a human life. Religion took these transitions seriously. It surrounded them with ceremony, with language honed over centuries, with the accumulated weight of tradition. It said: this moment matters. Pay attention. Be present. The secular equivalents are thin by comparison. A registry wedding. A funeral where nobody knows quite what to say. The celebrations of life that try to be cheerful and succeed mainly in being awkward. We have stripped the ritual from the transitions and left the transitions bare.

Moral conversation. Not moral instruction, I do not miss being told what to think about right and wrong. But the infrastructure for moral conversation. A shared vocabulary. A tradition of wrestling with ethical questions across centuries. The assumption that moral questions are important, that they deserve sustained attention, that a community should dedicate regular time to considering how to live well. The secular replacement is a culture war conducted on platforms optimised for outrage, where moral conversation has been reduced to moral performance and the goal is not understanding but victory.

Confrontation with death. This is the one I feel most personally, because chronic illness forces confrontation with mortality in ways that the culture around me has no framework for.

Religion made you practise thinking about death. Every week. It said: you will die. This life is finite. What comes after is uncertain, but this much is certain, it ends. And it provided a context for sitting with that knowledge, for processing it in community rather than alone, for developing some relationship with mortality that was not pure terror or pure denial.

Secular culture does not do this. It avoids death with a consistency that would be impressive if it were not so damaging. We have outsourced dying to hospitals and aged care facilities. We have removed it from the home and the community. We have created a culture that is terrified of death and has no language for it, no practice for it, no communal space in which to process it.

When my headaches are at their worst, when the pain reaches the level where the body's own calculus starts to weigh continuation against cessation, I discover that the culture around me has nothing to offer. No framework. No vocabulary. No ritual for sitting with the proximity of death and finding some footing within it. I have had to build my own, from scraps of philosophy and personal discipline and the sheer stubbornness of continuing.

III.

I think the loneliness epidemic, the meaning crisis, the rise of conspiracy thinking and pseudo-religious movements, these are not separate phenomena. They are symptoms of a single underlying condition. A civilisation that removed a load-bearing wall and did not install a replacement.

Religion was a load-bearing wall. It held up community, ritual, moral conversation, and the confrontation with death. When it was removed, gradually, over decades, through the entirely understandable process of Enlightenment rationality and scientific progress, the structures it supported did not remain standing. They sagged. They cracked. Some of them collapsed.

The conspiracy movements that have proliferated in recent years look, to me, like religions without the redeeming features. They provide community, the in-group, the shared knowledge, the sense of belonging to something larger. They provide narrative, the hidden truth, the battle between good and evil, the apocalyptic timeline. They provide meaning, your life matters because you are one of the few who sees the truth. They provide everything that religion provided except the things that made religion valuable: the emphasis on compassion, the discipline of humility, the confrontation with one's own fallibility, the moral seriousness that the best religious traditions cultivated over centuries.

The AI companions that are beginning to appear, chatbots designed for emotional connection, virtual friends, digital therapists, fill a similar void. They provide the feeling of being heard, of being known, of mattering to someone. They provide it without the friction and unpredictability of human relationship. They are, in a sense, secular confession without the confessor, pastoral care without the pastor.

I do not argue for a return to religion. You cannot believe what you do not believe. Faith is not a faucet you can turn on when you notice the plumbing is dry. The Enlightenment happened for good reasons. The scientific method is the most reliable tool for understanding reality that humans have ever developed. I would not give it back.

But I argue for taking seriously what was lost. For acknowledging that rationality alone is not sufficient for a full human life. For recognising that the needs religion served, for community, for ritual, for moral seriousness, for some relationship with death, are not religious needs. They are human needs. And they persist whether or not the metaphysical framework that once addressed them is credible.

The work of building secular structures that serve these needs has barely begun. We are a century into the secular experiment and we have not yet produced equivalents for what religion provided at its best. Perhaps we cannot. Perhaps these needs can only be met by frameworks that include some element of the transcendent, some gesture toward the larger, some willingness to engage with mystery.

If so, the challenge is to find forms of transcendence that do not require intellectual dishonesty. Forms of community that are regular and local and oriented toward something beyond the self. Rituals that mark the passages of life with the gravity they deserve. Moral conversations that are deeper than tribal signalling. A relationship with death that is neither denial nor despair.

The God-shaped hole is real. Pretending it is not, pretending that Amazon deliveries and Netflix and the scroll of the feed can fill it, is its own form of denial. We removed a load-bearing wall. The building is still standing, but the cracks are spreading, and people keep falling through the floor, and we keep being surprised when they do.

The building needs a new wall. I do not know what it looks like. I know it is not the old wall, and I know that pretending we do not need one is not working. The evidence is in the loneliness statistics, in the mental health data, in the conspiracy movements, in the quiet desperation of people who have everything the material world can provide and still feel that something essential is missing.

Something essential is missing. The honest response is to name it and begin, however awkwardly, to build.
