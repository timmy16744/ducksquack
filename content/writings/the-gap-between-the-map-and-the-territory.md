---
title: "The Gap Between the Map and the Territory"
date: "2023-04-15"
color: "red"
---

I want to talk about a specific kind of dishonesty. Not the kind where someone says something they know to be false. The kind where an entire society maintains, through institutional consensus, an official narrative that bears no relationship to what is actually happening.

Global carbon emissions reached a new record in 2022. Forty billion tonnes. The highest ever. This occurred in a year when the Paris Agreement was in effect, when net zero pledges had been announced by virtually every major economy, when the language of climate action was more prominent in policy documents and corporate communications than at any point in history.

The map says we are reducing emissions. The territory says we are increasing them. The gap between the two has become so wide that the map has ceased to be a map at all. It is a different document entirely, a statement of aspiration that bears roughly the same relationship to reality as a travel brochure bears to the destination it advertises.

I am not a climate denier. I want to be explicit about this because the discourse is structured so that any questioning of climate policy is immediately sorted into the denial category, and that sorting prevents the conversation I think needs to happen.

The conversation is not about whether climate change is real. It is. The conversation is not about whether human activity contributes. It does. The conversation is about honesty. About the gap between what we say we are doing and what we are actually doing, and about what happens to a civilisation that institutionalises the gap.

The Paris Agreement set a target of limiting warming to 1.5 degrees Celsius above pre-industrial levels. To achieve this, emissions needed to be cut roughly in half by 2030 and reach net zero by 2050. The target was announced with great ceremony. Pledges were made. Timelines were set. Press conferences were held.

Emissions have increased every year since.

The pledges are not being met. They are not close to being met. The gap between pledged reductions and actual emissions is not narrowing. It is widening. The International Energy Agency, the IPCC, the UNFCCC, the institutions whose job it is to track this, publish the numbers, and the numbers are clear, and the response to the numbers is another round of pledges.

This is not a failure of ambition. The pledges are ambitious. It is a failure of execution so comprehensive that the word failure does not quite capture it. Failure implies an attempt that fell short. What I observe is the systematic substitution of commitment for action, of announcement for implementation, of the map for the territory.

Australia is a particularly vivid illustration. We are among the highest per-capita emitters in the world. We have announced some of the most ambitious reduction targets. Our actual emissions trajectory has been, at best, flat. The gap between our stated ambition and our demonstrated behaviour is extraordinary.

We export coal to the world while pledging to reduce emissions. We approve new gas projects while committing to net zero. We set renewable energy targets and then allow grid connection delays to strand new capacity for years. The policy framework is not internally coherent because it is not designed to be coherent. It is designed to satisfy competing constituencies simultaneously: the environmental constituency that demands action and the economic constituency that demands continuation.

The result is a policy framework that is, in the precise sense of the word, fictional. It describes a reality that does not exist and is not being constructed. It functions not as a plan but as a performance, a demonstration, to domestic and international audiences, that the government is Doing Something, where the something is the announcement itself.

I find this more troubling than outright denial. A denier, at least, is making a claim that can be tested against evidence. The gap between the map and the territory is a different kind of failure, a failure of integrity so normalised that it no longer registers as dishonest. Everyone knows the targets will not be met. The officials who set them know. The modellers who assess them know. The diplomats who negotiate them know. The knowledge does not produce corrective action. It produces more sophisticated forms of the same performance.

The psychological function is clear. The targets allow people to feel that something is being done. The feeling is sufficient. Whether anything is actually being done is a separate question that the targets were not designed to answer.

This pattern, the substitution of performance for action, of announcement for implementation, of the map for the territory, is not unique to climate policy. It appears across Australian governance. Housing affordability targets that are announced and missed. Infrastructure timelines that are set and exceeded. Health system reform plans that are published and shelved. The pattern is so consistent that it constitutes, I think, a mode of governance, one in which the primary output is not policy but narrative, and the success of the narrative is measured not by outcomes but by reception.

The gap between the map and the territory is not a problem of communication or ambition. It is a problem of honesty. Until we are willing to acknowledge what we are actually doing, as opposed to what we are promising to do, the gap will continue to widen.

And the territory does not care about the map.
