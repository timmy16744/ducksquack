---
title: "The Zoom Class"
date: "2020-08-15"
color: "purple"
---

There are two pandemics. They happen in the same country, in the same cities, at the same time, but they are experienced by different people in ways so divergent that they might as well be occurring on different planets.

In one pandemic, life continues. It continues from home, which is inconvenient and sometimes lonely, but the work gets done. The laptop opens in the morning. The meetings happen on screens. The salary arrives. The mortgage gets paid. The children attend school online, which is suboptimal but manageable. The supermarket delivers. The streaming services provide entertainment. The pandemic is an interruption, a reconfiguration, perhaps even, for some, an improvement, no commute, more time at home, the flexibility that decades of requests to management never secured but a virus achieved in weeks.

In the other pandemic, you go to work or you do not eat. You stand at a register, you drive a truck, you stock a warehouse, you clean a hospital, you care for the elderly in aged care facilities where the virus kills with particular efficiency. You cannot work from home because your work is physical, located, embodied. Your job requires your body to be in a specific place, and that place is where other bodies are, and some of those bodies are infected.

The first group, the Zoom class, as I have started thinking of them, are largely insulated from the physical reality of the pandemic. They experience it as a disruption to convenience. The second group, the essential workers, the in-person economy, the people whose labour requires their presence, experience it as a disruption to their safety.

The language we use obscures this. We call them essential workers, which sounds like a compliment but functions as a designation. Essential means required. Required means you do not get to stay home. The word that sounds like respect is actually a command.

I have been watching this divide from Adelaide, where the lockdowns have been less severe than Melbourne but the dynamics are the same. The people I know in knowledge work have adapted. Some have thrived. The digital tools that enable remote work were already mature; the pandemic merely removed the institutional resistance to using them. Productivity, by many measures, has been maintained or even improved. The Zoom class has discovered that the office was, in many cases, unnecessary. The commute was wasted time. The open-plan layout was a productivity disaster that companies tolerated because it was cheaper than providing individual offices.

The people I know in physical work have not thrived. They have endured. The nurse who works extra shifts because colleagues are in isolation. The delivery driver who handles a hundred packages a day, each one potentially contaminated. The retail worker who enforces mask mandates on hostile customers for seventeen dollars an hour. These people are exhausted, stressed, underpaid relative to the risk they bear, and invisible to the Zoom class in the way that infrastructure is always invisible to the people who depend on it.

The division is permanent. This is what I want to argue, because the temporary framing allows everyone to believe that when the pandemic ends, the divide will heal. It will not. The pandemic has not created the divide. It has revealed and accelerated a divide that was already forming and will continue to widen.

The Zoom class will incorporate AI tools into their work. They will become more productive. Their skills will compound. They will work from anywhere, live where they choose, participate in a global labour market where talent commands a premium and location is irrelevant.

The physical workers will face a different trajectory. Automation will arrive, gradually and then suddenly, for the tasks that currently require their presence. Autonomous vehicles will displace drivers. Automated warehouses will displace pickers. Robotic systems will displace manufacturing workers. And when these people are displaced, the cognitive ladder that previous generations climbed, from manual work to administrative work to professional work, will not be available, because AI will already occupy the rungs.

The social contract between these two groups is dissolving. The Zoom class and the essential class inhabit the same country, pay taxes into the same system, vote in the same elections, but their daily realities have diverged so completely that the shared experience necessary for social cohesion is disappearing. They do not understand each other's lives. They do not share each other's risks. They increasingly do not inhabit the same economy.

In Australia, this maps onto geography. The inner cities, where knowledge workers cluster, are becoming islands of affluence and connectivity. The suburbs and regions, where physical work is concentrated, face a different set of pressures: stagnant wages, rising costs, declining services, the slow erosion of the institutions, clubs, churches, pubs, community organisations, that once provided social infrastructure.

Every society tells itself a story about shared fate. The Australian version of this story, the fair go, the idea that hard work leads to a reasonable life regardless of your starting point, has been under strain for decades. The pandemic has shown how thin the remaining threads are.

The Zoom class does not need the essential class to believe in the fair go. The essential class needs the fair go to be real. And the gap between the belief and the reality is growing at a rate that the pandemic has made impossible to ignore.

Whether anyone chooses to address it is another question. The Zoom class makes policy. The essential class makes the policy possible. The arrangement has a certain stability, so long as nobody examines it too closely. The pandemic forced an examination. What we do with what we have seen will determine whether the divide narrows or becomes permanent.

I suspect I know which it will be. But I would like to be wrong.
