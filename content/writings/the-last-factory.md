---
title: "The Last Factory"
date: "2017-10-15"
color: "orange"
---

Ford closed first. Then Holden announced. Then Toyota. By the time you read this, the last car will have rolled off the last Australian production line, and an industry that once employed hundreds of thousands of people will exist only in the past tense.

I want to sit with what this means, because I do not think we have sat with it yet. The coverage has been economic. Job losses tallied. Retraining programs announced. Transition packages costed. The language of closure has been managerial, as if a civilisational choice can be reduced to a spreadsheet.

It cannot. What happened here is not an economic event. It is a national character event. Australia decided, over the course of several decades, to stop making things. And now the decision is complete.

I.

Let me trace the logic, because the logic matters. It was not stupid. That is what makes it dangerous.

The argument went like this: Australia has high wages. High wages make manufacturing expensive. Countries with lower wages can manufacture the same goods more cheaply. Therefore, Australia should focus on what it does well, mining, agriculture, services, and let others handle the making of things. Comparative advantage. Ricardo. The textbook case.

And there is truth in it. Australian wages are high. A worker on an assembly line in Adelaide earns many times what an equivalent worker earns in Thailand or Mexico. The cars we produced were not cheaper or better than the ones produced elsewhere. Subsidies kept the industry alive long past the point where it could survive on its own economics.

So the subsidies were withdrawn, and the industry collapsed, and we told ourselves this was the market working as it should. Efficient allocation of resources. The invisible hand, doing its thing.

But here is what the textbook leaves out. An automotive industry is not a collection of car factories. It is an ecosystem. Ford, Holden, and Toyota were the visible canopy. Beneath them were a hundred and twenty supplier companies. Component manufacturers. Toolmakers. Specialists in metallurgy, in plastics, in precision engineering. Each one employing people who knew how to do things that most Australians have never thought about.

When the canopy falls, the understory dies. The comparison is literal. Those hundred and twenty companies did not transition to other industries. They closed. The toolmakers did not become app developers. They became redundant. The precision engineers did not retrain as baristas. They dispersed, taking with them knowledge that existed nowhere else in this country.

Knowledge is the part we do not discuss. The economic analysis counts jobs and dollars. It does not count the accumulated understanding of how to make things at scale. How to hold tolerances. How to manage a supply chain that delivers the right part to the right place at the right time, ten thousand times without failure. The knowledge cannot be written down or stored in a library. It is tacit, embodied, distributed across networks of people who have worked together for decades.

When those networks dissolve, the knowledge dissolves with them. You cannot reassemble it by posting a job advertisement. You cannot recreate it with a government grant. It is gone.

II.

Other countries faced the same pressures and made different choices.

Germany has high wages. Higher than ours, in many sectors. Germany has stringent environmental regulations, powerful unions, an expensive welfare state. By every measure that the Australian policy consensus uses to explain why manufacturing is impossible here, manufacturing should be impossible in Germany.

Germany manufactures. Enormously. Sophisticated products, exported globally, commanding premium prices. The automotive industry alone employs nearly eight hundred thousand people directly and several million more in the supply chain. They did not abandon manufacturing because labour was expensive. They moved up the value chain. They invested in automation, in precision, in the engineering culture that makes a Mercedes something a Camry is not.

Japan has almost no natural resources. It imports virtually everything it needs to make things. And yet Japan is one of the most formidable manufacturing nations on earth. The difference between Japan and Australia is not resources. We have resources Japan can only dream of. The difference is a cultural commitment to making things well, embedded in institutions and policy settings that support the commitment across generations.

South Korea was, within living memory, one of the poorest countries in Asia. It now produces automobiles, semiconductors, ships, and consumer electronics that compete with the best in the world. This did not happen by accident. It happened because the South Korean government decided, as a matter of national strategy, that manufacturing capability was worth building and protecting, even when the short-term economics argued against it.

We made the opposite decision. We decided that digging things up and shipping them elsewhere was sufficient. That services would fill the gap. That comparative advantage was not merely an economic theory but a national strategy.

I want to be precise about where I locate the responsibility. This was not inevitable. This was not the market operating according to natural law. This was policy. Decades of it. Bipartisan. Consistent. The decision to let manufacturing die was made by people, in offices, through the instruments of government, over a period of thirty years. At every point, alternative choices were available. At every point, examples of different approaches existed in countries with similar or higher cost structures. We chose this.

III.

What bothers me most is not the decision itself but the lack of seriousness with which it was made.

I have travelled enough to recognise the difference between a country that takes its productive capacity seriously and one that does not. In Japan, manufacturing is a source of national pride. The factory floor is respected. The craftsman is honoured. The ability to make things is understood, at a cultural level, as essential to national identity and security.

In Australia, manufacturing is an afterthought. A legacy sector. Something that other countries do, in places we would rather not visit, producing things we would rather not think about until they appear on a shelf. We have the mentality of consumers, not producers. We dig things up, we ship them away, and we buy back the finished products at a markup that would be comical if it were not so consequential.

Iron ore leaves our ports and returns as steel. Coal leaves and returns as electricity, embodied in the manufactured goods we import. Lithium departs and comes back as batteries. We are the quarry, and we have accepted the quarry's role with a complacency that I find increasingly difficult to understand.

The argument that this does not matter, that in a globalised world, it is rational to specialise and trade, depends on assumptions that are starting to look fragile. It assumes that supply chains will remain open. That trading partners will remain friendly. That the global order will remain stable. That we will never need to make things ourselves because there will always be someone willing to make them for us.

These assumptions have held for decades. They may hold for decades more. But they are assumptions, not laws. And a country that has dismantled its capacity to manufacture has also dismantled its capacity to respond if those assumptions fail.

IV.

There is a practical question here that I think deserves more attention than it receives. What does Australia actually make now?

Not what do we export, we export rocks and crops and education, and we are very good at it. But what do we make? What finished goods does this country produce? If the supply ships stopped arriving tomorrow, what could we provide for ourselves?

The answer is uncomfortable. We make remarkably little. We import most of our pharmaceuticals, most of our medical devices, most of our electronics, most of our vehicles, most of the components of the infrastructure we rely on daily. We are a wealthy country that is dependent on others for the physical stuff of civilisation.

The last car factory is closing. It will not be the last closure. It is the most visible one, the one that makes the news, but the erosion has been underway for decades and continues in sectors that attract less attention. Textiles. Chemicals. Plastics. Steel production shrinking. Processing capacity declining. Each closure treated as an isolated commercial decision rather than another data point in a pattern that should alarm anyone who thinks about national resilience.

I do not know what happens next. Perhaps the services economy will prove sufficient. Perhaps artificial intelligence and automation will create new forms of productive capacity that make traditional manufacturing obsolete. Perhaps the global order will remain stable enough that our dependence on foreign manufacturing never becomes a problem.

Perhaps. But I keep thinking about those hundred and twenty supplier companies. The toolmakers. The precision engineers. The people who knew how to make things, and who no longer have anywhere to use that knowledge. They are dispersing now, into other jobs or into retirement, and the things they knew are dispersing with them.

We chose this. We should at least be honest enough to look at what we chose.
