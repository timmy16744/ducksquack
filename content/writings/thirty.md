I turned thirty this week. People turn thirty every day. But I have been sitting with it, and what I find when I sit with it is not nostalgia or regret but something closer to impatience. A feeling that the shape of the first three decades was preparation for something, and that the something is arriving faster than the preparation anticipated.

I want to trace the shape honestly. Not the curated version. The actual one.


I grew up in Australia, which means I grew up inside an assumption. The assumption that things would continue. That the structures around you were permanent. That the world you inherited was, give or take, the world you would pass on. This assumption was embedded in everything: the suburban architecture, the school curriculum, the career advice, the national self-image. We were the lucky country. Luck was the plan.

The cluster headaches started when I was young enough that the medical system treated me as a curiosity rather than a patient. The medical literature calls them suicide headaches and that is clinical description, not hyperbole. I will not dwell on the pain here. I have written about it elsewhere and will write about it again. What matters for this essay is what the pain taught me, which is that systems fail.

Not occasionally. Not at the margins. Structurally. The Australian healthcare system failed me repeatedly across years. Wrong diagnoses. Wrong medications. Procedures that should never have been performed. Specialists who saw narrow slices of a person and never talked to each other. A system built for acute problems confronted with a chronic one, defaulting again and again to interventions that made things worse because intervening was what the system knew how to do.

I learned to build my own infrastructure. Exercise regimes, dietary controls, environmental management, the careful calibration of every input to a body that punishes miscalculation savagely. This was not wellness culture. This was survival engineering. And it gave me, against my will and at considerable cost, the most valuable education I possess: an intuition for how institutions actually work, as opposed to how they claim to work.

Most systems are held together by inertia and good intentions rather than competence and design. The people inside them are often individually capable and collectively useless. The gap between the brochure and the reality is where most of life actually happens. I learned this in waiting rooms and hospital corridors and the offices of specialists who charged four hundred dollars to be confidently wrong. It has proved applicable to nearly everything else.


My twenties. I will be honest about these because that is the only version worth writing.

I built an expertise in product and design thinking. The intersection of technology and human behaviour. How people interact with systems. Why some products feel inevitable and others feel forced. The thousand small decisions that separate something that works from something that merely functions. I gravitated toward this because I could see, earlier than most people around me, that technology was going to restructure everything. Not in the Silicon Valley poster sense. In the structural, irreversible, civilisational sense. The way electricity restructured everything. The way internal combustion restructured everything. I wanted to understand how humans would live on the other side of it.

I was right about the direction. I was wrong about the timeline. I thought I had decades to position myself. I had years. The acceleration in artificial intelligence over the past twenty-four months has compressed what I expected to be a generational transformation into something happening now, in real time, faster than anyone I know can fully process.

But the twenties were not only work and pain management. I need to say this because the illness can become the whole story if you let it, and I have fought hard not to let it.

I travelled. Forty-five countries before this birthday. Not backpacking in the gap-year sense. Something more deliberate. Each country is a different answer to the same human questions: how do we organise ourselves, what do we build, what do we value, what do we neglect? You cannot understand your own country until you have seen enough others to know what is local and what is universal. Australians particularly need this education, because the comfort and isolation of this continent breeds a provincialism that masquerades as worldliness simply because we speak English and watch American television.

I fell in love with Japan. Multiple times, across multiple visits, each one deepening rather than diminishing the fascination. It is difficult to explain because it is not one thing. It is the totality. A culture that treats the making of a bowl of ramen with the seriousness other cultures reserve for constitutional law. A society of 125 million people that functions with an efficiency and mutual consideration that would be unrecognisable in Sydney. An aesthetic sensibility so pervasive that a convenience store is better designed than most Australian restaurants. The word kodawari, which translates poorly but means something like an uncompromising commitment to getting things right. Coming home from Tokyo is an exercise in recalibrating your expectations downward. Japan has almost no natural resources and has built something extraordinary. Australia has almost every natural resource and has built something adequate. That gap is not material. It is cultural. It is the difference between a society that demands excellence and one that has learned to accept good enough.

Travel taught me this. It is, I think, the most important education available to anyone who wants to understand the world rather than merely inhabit it.


Here is what I believe at thirty, stated without the diplomatic softening I have learned to apply in conversation.

The next three years will be more consequential than the last thirty. Not for me personally, though they may be that too. For the world. For Australia. For the basic arrangements that govern how people live and work and organise themselves.

I believe this because I can see the curves, and the curves do not care about political cycles or institutional comfort.

Solar photovoltaic costs have fallen ninety-nine percent since 1976. The learning curve is relentless: every doubling of cumulative production reduces costs by twenty to twenty-five percent. We are approaching the point where solar plus storage is cheaper than the marginal cost of running existing fossil fuel plants. Not building new ones. Running the ones we already have. When energy becomes effectively free at the margin, everything downstream of energy becomes radically cheaper. Manufacturing. Transport. Computation. Agriculture. Desalination. The entire cost structure of civilisation sits downstream of energy.

Autonomous vehicles work. They work well enough that in controlled environments they already outperform human drivers on every safety metric that matters. The technology is not the bottleneck. Liability frameworks are the bottleneck. Insurance models are the bottleneck. Union agreements are the bottleneck. The political influence of industries that will be displaced is the bottleneck.

Large language models can already perform the core cognitive tasks of a junior lawyer, a junior accountant, a junior analyst. Not perfectly. Not with seasoned judgment. But competently enough, and cheaply enough, that the economic logic of hiring a human for those tasks is eroding in real time. Within three years the capabilities will be substantially greater and the displacement will be undeniable.

Nuclear energy could transform this country's power grid inside a decade if we chose to allow it. Small modular reactors are being deployed elsewhere. The technology is proven. The safety record is extraordinary. We sit on the largest uranium reserves on earth. We will not do this because four words in legislation prohibit it and no politician possesses the spine to delete them.

Telemedicine could deliver specialist care to every rural community in Australia. The technology exists. It was demonstrated during COVID lockdowns. It works. It is not deployed because regulatory frameworks were designed for physical consulting rooms and paper files, and nobody with authority has bothered to update them.

Gene therapies for conditions that have been intractable for generations are in advanced trials. CRISPR-based treatments are crawling through approval processes designed for small-molecule drugs in the 1960s. Treatments that could be helping patients in months are taking years because the regulatory apparatus cannot distinguish between a pill and a technology that edits the human genome. The caution is not unreasonable in principle. In practice it is killing people through delay.

I could continue. Prefabricated housing. Automated manufacturing. Drone logistics. Desalination at scale. In every case the technology is ready or nearly ready, and in every case the bottleneck is the same: regulation written for a world that no longer exists, administered by institutions whose primary function has become self-perpetuation.


I want to say something about Australia specifically, and I want to say it without the qualifications I would normally attach.

We are a country that has been lucky for so long we have confused luck with competence. The resources were in the ground. The geography was favourable. The American alliance provided security we did not pay for. Proximity to Asia provided markets we did not cultivate. We rode these advantages for decades and told ourselves it was skill.

The luck is running out. Not because the resources are depleted. We have more lithium, uranium, iron ore, solar exposure, and agricultural land than almost any nation on earth. The raw materials for the next century of human civilisation are under our feet and falling on our rooftops.

The luck is running out because the institutional capacity to convert those advantages into anything useful is decaying faster than the advantages can compensate.

I am going to say something that will sound inflammatory. I believe it to be straightforwardly true. The combination of regulatory sclerosis and union entrenchment will destroy this country's capacity to navigate the transformation that is coming, unless something changes fundamentally and soon.

I say this without ideological attachment. Regulation serves legitimate purposes. Unions have played essential historical roles. I understand the history. I respect the achievements. What I observe in 2022 is not regulation serving safety or unions protecting workers. What I observe is regulation serving incumbency and unions protecting obsolescence.

I have watched it personally. I have sat in planning meetings where a project that should take months was mapped onto a timeline of years because every conceivable objection had been encoded into process. Environmental assessment. Heritage review. Community consultation. Traffic study. Each one individually defensible. Collectively, paralysing. A process that takes months in Tokyo or Seoul takes years here. Not because our buildings are safer or our cities are better planned. They are not. But because the accumulated weight of procedural requirements has made delay the primary output of the system.

Construction unions operate with a degree of influence that would astonish observers from most other developed economies. Work rules that prevent adoption of labour-saving technology. Demarcation disputes that fragment simple tasks into complex multi-trade operations. Enterprise agreements that lock in cost structures regardless of productivity. I have watched projects where the technology existed to do the work faster, cheaper, and safer, and the work was done slowly, expensively, and dangerously instead, because the agreements required it.

Every dollar of unnecessary cost in construction is a dollar added to housing prices. Every year of delay in infrastructure is a year of foregone benefit. The people who pay are not the regulators or the union officials. They are the young Australians who cannot afford a house. They are the communities waiting decades for transport that other countries deliver in years. They are the businesses that look at the approvals timeline and invest somewhere else.

Energy is worse. Coal is politically toxic. Nuclear is legally banned. Gas is entangled in moratoriums. Renewables face grid connection delays and planning objections that stall projects for years. We are a continent with the most abundant energy resources on earth and some of the highest electricity prices in the developed world. This is not paradox. This is policy.

And now artificial intelligence arrives, and with it the most significant economic transformation since industrialisation. The countries that move fastest will capture disproportionate value. The countries that move slowest will become dependencies of the ones that moved first.

I look at our regulatory apparatus and our institutional culture, and I see a country that is structurally incapable of moving fast.


I think about South Africa, and I think about it more often than I would like.

The comparison is not exact. South Africa's trajectory was shaped by a specific and brutal history that Australia does not share. But the structural parallel is instructive in ways that should make us deeply uncomfortable.

South Africa is a country with extraordinary natural resources. Minerals, agricultural land, strategic geography. It had a functioning industrial base, sophisticated financial institutions, competent infrastructure. It was not destined to become what it is becoming.

What happened was not a single catastrophe. It was a slow degradation of institutional capacity. State capture. Regulatory dysfunction. The progressive hollowing out of the people and processes that kept critical systems running. And, most relevantly: an energy crisis.

Eskom. Load shedding. The euphemistic term for rolling blackouts that have become a feature of daily life. Businesses running on generators. Hospitals on backup power. An economy haemorrhaging billions annually to energy unreliability. Investment fleeing to jurisdictions where the lights stay on.

The descent took two decades. Underinvestment, corruption, skills erosion, failure to build new generation capacity while demand grew. The warning signs were visible for years. They were ignored, acknowledged and not acted upon, or addressed with policies that treated symptoms while deepening causes.

Now imagine Australia in fifteen years if we continue on our present course.

We have banned our most reliable form of baseload power. We are decommissioning coal plants on a political timetable that bears no relationship to the readiness of replacement capacity. We are building renewables, but grid connection delays mean new capacity sits idle for years after construction. Battery storage at the required scale does not exist on current deployment timelines. Gas, which could bridge the gap, is entangled in export disputes and domestic reservation politics. The ExxonMobil refinery at Altona closed last year and took a chunk of downstream industry with it. The aluminium smelters are under constant pressure from energy costs. Manufacturers are watching their margins evaporate and starting to ask whether it is worth staying.

When Ford, Holden, and Toyota left in 2017, we told ourselves it was the inevitable cost of high wages and a small domestic market. We did not ask whether the policy settings that made those departures inevitable were the right policy settings. We did not ask what it meant that a hundred and twenty supplier companies went with them. We did not ask what happens to a country that stops making things. We told ourselves services would fill the gap, that the knowledge economy would provide, that we could dig things up and ship them somewhere else and somehow that would be enough.

It will not be enough.

If we do not build new baseload capacity, and do it quickly, we face a future where energy scarcity becomes the defining constraint on Australian economic life. Not in the abstract sense. In the practical, daily, South African sense. Rolling shortages. Industrial closures. Capital flight. The grinding erosion of living standards we have treated as a birthright.

This is where the regulatory and union problem stops being expensive and starts being existential. Building energy infrastructure requires speed. Our system is designed for process. Process measured in years. Process that can be challenged, appealed, reviewed, and relitigated at every stage by anyone with standing and a lawyer.

The unions will resist automation of energy infrastructure. They will resist changes to work practices. They will resist developments that threaten existing employment arrangements, even when those arrangements are incompatible with the energy system the country needs. This is not speculation. It is extrapolation from observed behaviour applied to foreseeable circumstances.

If the correct leadership does not emerge, if we do not find people capable of making difficult decisions about regulatory reform and institutional modernisation, Australia could find itself in a position that would have seemed unthinkable a generation ago. A resource-rich nation unable to keep the lights on. A country with the raw materials for energy abundance choosing, through institutional paralysis, energy scarcity.

Mad Max was set in Australia for a reason. George Miller understood something about the fragility of civilisation on a continent this vast and this harsh. Strip away the infrastructure and you are left with distance, heat, and competition for what remains. We are not close to that scenario. But we are closer than we were ten years ago, and the direction of travel should concern anyone paying attention.


This is supposed to be a birthday essay. I have spent most of it angry. I notice this and I am not going to apologise for it.

For most of my twenties I modulated. I softened the edges of what I could see because unmodulated observation makes people uncomfortable. You sound alarmist when you are being precise. You sound pessimistic when you are being realistic. You learn to present observations in forms people can receive without defensiveness, and in the process you dilute the observations until they lose their force.

I am done modulating.

I have spent thirty years learning how the world actually works. Through building products, through navigating broken institutions with my own body as the test case, through walking the streets of forty-five countries and asking why this place works and that one does not. I know what functional systems look like. I have experienced them in Tokyo and Singapore and Copenhagen. I know what dysfunctional systems look like. I have experienced them in Australian hospitals and planning departments and energy markets.

The next three years will see artificial intelligence transform knowledge work beyond recognition. Energy economics will shift in ways that redistribute geopolitical power. Autonomous systems will move from demonstration to deployment. The gap between countries that adapt and countries that stall will widen into a chasm that takes generations to cross.

The technology arriving is not the threat. The technology is a gift. Cheaper energy, smarter tools, longer and healthier lives, problems solved that have resisted solution for generations. All of it within reach. All of it possible within my lifetime, perhaps within this decade.

The threat is us. Our institutions. Our regulations. Our unions. Our reflexive preference for the familiar over the functional. Our political class that cannot understand the internet, let alone what comes after it. Our national habit of confusing process with progress and consultation with action.

I am thirty. I have a condition that will not relent and a mind that will not stop noticing. I have seen enough of the world to know what is possible and enough of Australia to know what we are choosing instead.

The next three years will be more transformative than the last thirty. Everything that came before was the prologue. The rate of change is about to make the prologue look like it was standing still.

I am ready for it. I am not sure this country is. But I intend to find out, and I do not intend to be quiet about what I see.
