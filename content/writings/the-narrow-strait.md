---
title: "The Narrow Strait"
date: "2019-05-10"
color: "blue"
---

I have been looking at maps lately. Not the political maps that show borders and capitals and the colourful fiction of national sovereignty, but the other kind. The kind that shows what actually moves.

Trade routes. Shipping lanes. The physical corridors through which the global economy flows. When you look at these maps, you notice something that the political maps conceal: the entire system passes through a handful of very narrow places.

The Strait of Hormuz. Thirty-three kilometres wide at its narrowest point. Through it flows roughly twenty percent of the world's oil supply. Iran is on one side. Oman and the UAE on the other. Tankers navigate this passage every day, and every day the global economy depends on their safe passage.

The Strait of Malacca. At its narrowest, less than three kilometres of navigable channel. Through it passes roughly a quarter of all global trade by volume. Virtually everything that moves between the Indian Ocean and the Pacific, oil from the Gulf to East Asia, manufactured goods from China to Europe, funnels through this corridor between Malaysia and Indonesia.

The Suez Canal. An artificial strait, but a chokepoint nonetheless. Roughly twelve percent of global trade. When the Ever Given ran aground in 2021, I am writing before that happened, but the scenario has been war-gamed extensively, it will demonstrate how quickly a single obstruction in a single channel can cascade through the entire global economy.

The Taiwan Strait. Narrower in strategic significance than in physical geography. Taiwan produces the vast majority of the world's advanced semiconductors. The chips in your phone, your car, your medical equipment, your infrastructure, they were fabricated in facilities on an island that sits in contested waters between the United States and China.

The Bosphorus. The Panama Canal. The Danish Straits. The list continues, but the pattern is clear. Globalisation, the system that provides nearly everything we consume, use, and depend on, passes through geographical needles. And the needles are narrow.

I find this fascinating and alarming in roughly equal measure.

Fascinating because it reveals the physical reality beneath the economic abstraction. We talk about globalisation as if it were a system of information, a network of financial flows, something that exists in spreadsheets and databases. It is not. It is a physical system. Ships on water. Fuel in tanks. Steel containers stacked on decks, navigating passages that have been strategically significant since humans first put cargo on boats. The geography has not changed since the Phoenicians. Only the stakes have.

Alarming because nobody prices this fragility. The global economy operates as if these passages will remain open. Shipping costs are calculated on the assumption of uninterrupted transit. Supply chains are designed for efficiency, not resilience. The just-in-time manufacturing philosophy, which has been adopted by nearly every industry on earth, assumes that components will arrive when they are needed, which assumes that the ships carrying them will traverse the straits without delay.

What happens when they do not?

The honest answer is that nobody is entirely sure, because it has not happened at a significant scale in the modern era. There have been disruptions. The closure of the Suez Canal from 1967 to 1975 rerouted trade around Africa and reshaped shipping economics. Piracy in the Strait of Malacca has occasionally increased insurance costs and required naval patrols. But a simultaneous closure of one of the major chokepoints, a military conflict in the Taiwan Strait, a confrontation in Hormuz, a blockade of the Malacca, would be unprecedented in the era of integrated global supply chains.

For Australia, this is not abstract. We are an island continent. Everything we do not produce domestically arrives by sea. And the sea lanes through which it arrives pass through the most contested geographical corridors on earth.

Our imports from Northeast Asia, manufactured goods, electronics, machinery, transit the South China Sea and the approaches to the Taiwan Strait. Our energy imports, to the extent we have them, come through passages that are subject to the strategic interests of nations whose priorities may not align with ours. Our exports, iron ore, coal, agricultural products, follow the same routes in reverse.

We have built an economy that depends utterly on maritime trade flowing uninterrupted through straits that we do not control, cannot defend independently, and have no plan to do without.

This dependence is not a secret. Strategists and defence planners are aware of it. The vulnerability appears in white papers and strategic assessments with the regularity of an annual report. But awareness has not produced action. We have not built domestic manufacturing capacity that could substitute for interrupted imports. We have not diversified our trade routes to reduce dependence on any single corridor. We have not invested in the kind of strategic reserves, fuel, medical supplies, critical components, that would buffer the economy against a significant disruption.

Every previous era of globalisation ended. The Mediterranean trade networks of the ancient world. The Silk Road. The British Empire's global trading system. Each was disrupted or destroyed by conflict, by shifting power, by the closure of the routes that sustained it. There is no law of nature that makes our version permanent.

I do not predict disruption. Prediction is a fool's game at this level of complexity. What I observe is a system that assumes continuity in conditions where continuity is not guaranteed, and a country that has made itself extraordinarily dependent on that assumption without building any fallback for the possibility that the assumption is wrong.

The straits are narrow. The ships are large. The consequences of obstruction are severe. And we proceed as if the passages will remain open because they have always been open, which is the kind of reasoning that works perfectly until the moment it does not.
