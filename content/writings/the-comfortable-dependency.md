---
title: "The Comfortable Dependency"
date: "2023-11-15"
color: "orange"
---

I want to map something. Not a geographic map but a dependency map. A diagram of what Australia depends on, who provides it, and what happens if the provision stops.

Start with security. Our strategic architecture is American. The ANZUS treaty. The Pine Gap facility. The interoperability of our military forces with American systems. The intelligence sharing of the Five Eyes alliance. The nuclear submarines we are acquiring through AUKUS, which we will not be able to build, maintain, or operate without ongoing American support. Our defence is not independent and reinforced by alliance. It is dependent on alliance in the way that a building is dependent on its foundations.

Move to trade. Our largest export partner is China. Iron ore, coal, gas, agricultural products, education services. The revenue from this trade funds a significant portion of government expenditure. The relationship has been strained, the barley tariffs, the wine restrictions, the diplomatic tensions that followed the call for a COVID inquiry, but the iron ore trade has continued because the mutual dependence is deep enough to survive political friction. For now.

Move to technology. The platforms we use are American. The cloud infrastructure our businesses depend on is American. The AI systems that are beginning to reshape our economy are developed in American and, increasingly, Chinese labs. We have no domestic platform of significance. No cloud infrastructure at scale. No AI capability that registers on the global scoreboard. Our digital economy runs on infrastructure we do not own, cannot influence, and would be unable to replace.

Move to manufacturing. We import the vast majority of our manufactured goods. Electronics from China. Vehicles from Japan and Korea and Thailand. Pharmaceuticals from India and Europe. Medical devices from the United States and Germany. The things that fill our hospitals, power our businesses, and sustain our daily lives come from somewhere else.

Move to energy. We export energy in raw form, coal, gas, uranium, and import it in refined form, embedded in the manufactured goods and processed materials we buy back. Our domestic energy market is fragile, as I have written elsewhere, with prices rising and reliability declining while we sit on some of the most abundant energy resources on earth.

Move to food. We are a food exporter, which provides some security. But the inputs to food production, fertiliser, agricultural chemicals, machinery, fuel, are substantially imported. The farm produces food. The inputs that enable the farm to produce food come from elsewhere.

Now step back and look at the map.

Security: American. Trade: Chinese. Technology: American. Manufacturing: Chinese, Japanese, Korean, European. Energy: structurally dependent on foreign processing and infrastructure. The word that describes this map is not alliance or partnership or integration. The word is dependency. And dependency, at the scale and comprehensiveness that characterises Australia's position, is a form of vulnerability that no amount of diplomatic language can disguise.

I have visited countries that take sovereignty seriously. Singapore maintains a military capability that is formidable relative to its size, a domestic technology sector that provides alternatives to foreign platforms, a strategic reserve system that buffers against supply disruption, and a governance culture that treats self-reliance as a national priority. Israel builds its own weapons, develops its own technology, produces its own intelligence. The Nordic countries invest in domestic capability even when it would be cheaper to import, because they understand that cheapness purchased through dependency is not cheap at all.

Australia does none of this. We optimise for cost. We buy the cheapest option from the most convenient supplier and call it efficiency. We outsource our security to America, our trade to China, our technology to Silicon Valley, and our manufacturing to everyone else, and we call the resulting arrangement an economy. It is an economy. It is also a web of dependencies so comprehensive that the concept of sovereignty becomes, in practical terms, a polite fiction.

What does sovereign mean when you cannot fuel your own economy for more than a few weeks without foreign supply? When your military cannot operate without foreign systems? When your businesses cannot function without foreign cloud infrastructure? When your hospitals cannot treat patients without foreign pharmaceuticals and devices?

It means very little. It means you have a flag and an anthem and a seat at the United Nations. It does not mean you control the systems your civilisation depends on. Because you do not.

The comfort is the problem. We are comfortable. The dependencies have not yet been tested by a serious disruption. The supply chains function. The alliance holds. The trade continues. Everything works, the way a building with hidden structural flaws works, perfectly, right up until the moment it does not.

The world that permitted this particular comfort, the world of stable American hegemony, open trading systems, reliable supply chains, and benign geopolitical competition, is not the world that is emerging. The emerging world is contested, fragmented, and increasingly organised around blocs that do not guarantee access to non-members.

If we enter that world carrying the dependency map I have described, we enter it as a client state, dependent on the goodwill of others for the basic necessities of modern existence. The goodwill may persist. It may not. The point is that we have no fallback if it does not, and we have spent the decades in which a fallback could have been built choosing comfort instead.

The comfortable dependency has worked for seventy years. The question is whether the next seventy will resemble the last. The evidence suggests they will not. And the time to prepare for a different world is before the current one ends, not after.
