---
title: "The Argument from Pain"
date: "2020-07-15"
color: "purple"
---

Three o'clock in the morning. Adelaide is silent. The house is dark. The only thing that exists is the pain, which has settled behind my right eye with the focused intensity of a blowtorch applied to the interior of my skull.

Cluster headaches. The medical literature describes them as among the most painful conditions known to humanity. The nickname is suicide headaches, and the nickname is not metaphorical. It is descriptive. People kill themselves during these attacks, not because they have lost the will to live in any philosophical sense but because the pain is so extreme that the body's calculus flips: death becomes preferable to continuation. This is a physical computation, not a moral one.

I am in month six of the longest lockdown in Australian history, or rather, Melbourne is; Adelaide's restrictions are lighter but the isolation is real, and the solitude has given me time with questions I usually keep in motion. When you are busy, you can outrun the questions. When the world stops and the pain arrives and it is three in the morning and there is nothing between you and the question, you cannot outrun it.

The question is this: if there is a designer, my nervous system is the counter-argument.

I want to engage with this honestly, which means I need to say where I start. I grew up in Adelaide, attended Prince Alfred College, a school with a Uniting Church affiliation and chapel services that I attended regularly without believing much of what was said in them. I was not raised in a religious household. God was not a presence in my home. He was a presence at school, in the architecture and the hymns and the solemn language of assembly, and I found the architecture beautiful and the hymns moving and the language interesting without ever quite believing that there was someone on the other end of the line.

Atheism strikes me as a claim of certainty that the evidence does not support. Whether there is a God, a designer, a consciousness behind the machinery of the universe. Nobody knows. The honest position, the position that respects both the limits of human knowledge and the genuine strangeness of existence, is uncertainty. I am agnostic, and I hold the agnosticism seriously, not as a polite hedge but as an intellectual commitment. I do not have enough information to be certain, and pretending otherwise in either direction seems to me a failure of honesty.

What I can say, with the authority of direct experience, is that the existence of pain of this magnitude poses a problem for any theology that begins with a benevolent designer.

Let me take the arguments seriously, because they deserve to be taken seriously. The people who hold them are not stupid. Many are considerably smarter than I am. The theological tradition that wrestles with suffering is thousands of years old, and it has produced responses of genuine depth and sophistication.

Free will. The argument that a universe capable of genuine moral agency must also be capable of suffering, because freedom requires the possibility of harm. This is coherent. I understand it. But cluster headaches are not the result of anyone's free will. They are a neurological malfunction, a misfiring of the trigeminal nerve, a biological error that has no moral dimension. Nobody chose this. Nobody's freedom is enhanced by it. The free will defence addresses moral suffering, the suffering caused by human choices, and has nothing to say about natural suffering, which is the suffering I know best.

Growth through adversity. The argument that suffering builds character, deepens empathy, forges strength. This is the argument I find most tempting and most dangerous. Tempting because there is truth in it, I have learned things through pain that I could not have learned any other way. Dangerous because it comes perilously close to justifying suffering, to suggesting that the pain was necessary, even good, because of what it produced.

I reject this. Not because it is entirely wrong but because the proportionality is obscene. You do not need a blowtorch to teach someone the value of warmth. You do not need the most painful condition in medical literature to develop empathy. If there is a teacher behind this curriculum, the teacher is either incompetent or sadistic, and in either case does not deserve the gratitude that the growth-through-adversity argument implies.

The mystery. The argument that God's purposes are beyond human comprehension, that suffering serves a function we cannot see from our limited perspective, that the design is too large to be judged by creatures as small as us. This is the most honest of the theological responses, because it acknowledges the problem without pretending to solve it. But it is also the most empty, because it can justify anything. Any amount of suffering, any degree of cruelty, any injustice, can be waved away with the claim that God's purposes are mysterious. The argument amounts to a surrender dressed as humility.

I have sat with these arguments at three in the morning, when the pain is at its worst and the philosophical distance between suffering and theodicy has collapsed to zero. In those moments, the arguments are not interesting. They are insulting. Not because they are wrong in their internal logic but because they ask me to value the explanation more than the experience. To trust the brochure when the product is demonstrably broken.

And I have spent enough time navigating broken systems, healthcare systems, institutional systems, the slow machinery of Australian bureaucracy, to know what that particular form of trust costs. It costs your ability to see clearly. It costs your capacity for honest assessment. It costs, ultimately, your grip on reality, because reality is the thing in front of you, not the explanation offered for it.

But I want to say something else, something that complicates the picture, because the picture deserves complication.

The same nervous system that produces the agony also produces everything else. The sunset in Kyoto that stopped me mid-step and held me there, breathless, while the light turned the temple gardens into something that felt more real than real. The music that sometimes, for reasons I cannot explain, makes the world briefly cohere into something that feels like meaning. The taste of food prepared by someone who cares about the preparation. The experience of understanding something for the first time, the click of comprehension, the moment when a pattern that was invisible becomes visible and the world rearranges itself around the new seeing.

These experiences come from the same apparatus. The same neurons. The same chemistry that tortures me also gives me everything worth having. None of this points to design. If anything, it points to indifference. The system was not built for me. It was not built for anything. It is a system that processes, that responds, that generates experience as a byproduct of complexity, and the experience includes both agony and beauty because the system does not distinguish between them. It produces both with equal facility and no apparent preference.

I find this strangely liberating. If the system is indifferent, then the suffering is not a message, not a test, not a lesson, not the hand of God pressing me into shape. Just pain. And pain, stripped of meaning, is easier to bear than pain that is supposed to mean something, because meaningful pain carries the additional burden of interpretation. Why me? What is the lesson? What am I supposed to learn? These questions, which are natural and understandable, add a layer of suffering on top of the suffering itself. Remove the meaning, and you remove the layer.

I cannot claim there is a God. I cannot claim there is not. What I can claim is that I do not know, and that the not-knowing is the honest position, and that from within the not-knowing it is possible to build a life that is meaningful even if the meaning is constructed rather than received.

The values I hold, the importance of honesty, of compassion, of treating others with dignity, of pursuing understanding for its own sake, these are not unique to any religion, but they are present in most of them. Christianity offers them. Hinduism offers them. The secular humanist tradition offers them. I take them where I find them and I do not worry much about the metaphysical framework they arrive in. A good value is a good value regardless of whether it was delivered by a prophet or a philosopher or a parent or the hard experience of suffering that teaches you, against your will, what it means to need kindness and not receive it.

At three in the morning, with the pain at its peak and the world reduced to the space between my skull and the pillow, theology is not a comfort. What is a comfort, to the extent that anything is, is simpler: the knowledge that the pain will end. Not permanently. But this attack will end, and when it does, the world will still be there, and in the world there will be things worth experiencing, and the experiencing is the point. Not the explanation for the experience. Not the cosmic framework. The thing itself. The sunset. The music. The taste of food. The click of understanding.

Meaning is not given. It is built. By hand. In pain. Without guarantee. And the building is the thing.
